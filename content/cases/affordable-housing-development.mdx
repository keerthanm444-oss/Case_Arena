## The situation

Affordable housing production is one of the most-discussed public-policy challenges in US states. The economic structure — capital-intensive, slow, dependent on federal tax credits + state subsidies + private capital — creates inherent production constraints. The political structure — local zoning control, community opposition, NIMBY dynamics — adds further friction.

Tripling production over 10 years requires both more capital and more efficient deployment of existing capital.

## Structuring

Three branches:

1. **Production typologies** — new construction (highest cost per unit), preservation of existing buildings (faster, lower cost), conversion of underutilized commercial space, accessory dwelling units (ADUs).
2. **Capital mix** — LIHTC, state subsidy, private debt + equity, federal HUD programs, partnerships with employers / hospitals / universities.
3. **Process levers** — zoning reform, permitting acceleration, modular construction, public land utilization.

## Key analysis

Production economics by typology:

| Typology | Cost per unit | Production time | Capital efficiency |
|----------|---------------|-----------------|---------------------|
| New mid-rise | $350-500K | 36-48 months | Baseline |
| Preservation/rehab | $150-250K | 18-24 months | 2-3x more units per $ |
| Commercial conversion | $250-350K | 24-30 months | 1.5-2x |
| ADU | $100-200K | 12-18 months | High but scattered |

The non-obvious finding: **preservation + conversion can produce 2-3x more units per public dollar than new construction.** If political pressure focuses on visible new buildings, capital is misallocated.

Three additional levers compound:
- **Public land utilization:** state/city-owned land contributed reduces land cost (often 15-25% of project cost)
- **Modular construction:** can reduce cost 15-25% and time 30-40%
- **Process acceleration:** by-right approvals in transit corridors can save 12-18 months per project

## Recommendation skeleton

**Recommendation:** **Multi-typology production strategy over 10 years.** New construction: 40% of units (12,000 units). Preservation: 30% (9,000). Conversion: 20% (6,000). ADU + scattered-site: 10% (3,000). Capital deployment: state subsidy + LIHTC + employer partnerships + modular pilots. Pair with zoning + permitting reform (by-right in transit corridors, expanded ADU rights). Annual production ramp: 1,500 → 2,500 → 3,500 → 4,000+ units.

**Three supports:**

1. Multi-typology approach maximizes units per public dollar.
2. Process + zoning reforms compound the capital impact through faster development cycles.
3. Phased production ramp builds capacity gradually rather than overwhelming the development ecosystem.

**One risk:** community opposition delays projects despite zoning reforms. Mitigate by community engagement + transparent affordability targeting + visible benefit-sharing (e.g., neighborhood improvements alongside new housing).

## What judges look for

- Did you propose **multi-typology** rather than all-new-construction?
- Did you size **preservation/conversion** as more capital-efficient?
- Did you address **zoning + permitting reform** as production accelerators?
- Did you size **public land + employer partnerships** as creative funding sources?
