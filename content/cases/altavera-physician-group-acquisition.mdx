## The situation

Health system acquisition of physician groups is one of the dominant US healthcare M&A patterns over the past 15 years. The strategic logic is real (referral capture, value-based care positioning, ambulatory care expansion). The execution risk is also real and often underestimated (physician departure, cultural integration, financial dilution).

The CFO's concern is correct. The CEO's strategic logic is also correct. The case is whether the specific deal works.

Three branches that must all be yes:

1. **Strategic fit** — does owning this group make Altavera materially stronger? Referral lock-in, ambulatory care portfolio, value-based care contracting leverage.
2. **Valuation** — is $850M defensible? At 10x EBITDA and ~6-7% pretax margin, the implied physician-group revenue is ~$1.4B.
3. **Integration risk** — can Altavera actually realize the synergies, retain physicians, and manage the operational integration?

If any of the three is no, the deal doesn't work regardless of how attractive the other two are.

## Key analysis

### Strategic fit

Pre-acquisition, the 400-physician group sends 60-70% of referrals to Altavera hospitals (historical pattern). Acquisition would lock the remaining 30-40% — incremental admissions of 2,000-3,000 per year, worth roughly $40-70M in additional hospital revenue.

Plus value-based care positioning: a system with employed primary care + specialists can take risk-based contracts that pure hospital systems cannot.

Verdict: strategic fit is real, but the incremental referral capture is smaller than headline implies — most was already happening.

### Valuation

10x EBITDA on a physician group is at the high end of current market multiples; recent comparable transactions have been 8-12x. Whether $850M is defensible depends on:

- Synergies — can Altavera grow the group's EBITDA?
- Cost of capital — Altavera's WACC determines acceptable payback period.
- Alternative uses — what else could $850M do?

Verdict: $850M is high but not unreasonable in a competitive bidding context.

### Integration risk

The hard branch. Typical physician-group acquisition risks:

- **Physician departure** — 15-30% leave within 3 years of acquisition. The departures are usually the highest-revenue specialists.
- **Productivity decline** — employed physicians often work 5-15% fewer hours than they did as partners.
- **Operational integration cost** — Altavera doesn't have current operating capability for ambulatory care at this scale.

Net of these risks, EBITDA realization is typically 60-80% of pre-acquisition projection. At 60% realization on $85M EBITDA = $51M, the implied multiple is 16x. The valuation breaks down quickly under realistic integration assumptions.

## Recommendation skeleton

**Recommendation:** Counter-offer at $720M with explicit physician-retention bonuses funded from the deal proceeds. If the seller refuses, walk away and propose a long-term clinical-affiliation agreement instead. Acquisition only makes sense if (a) seller accepts lower price and (b) Altavera builds physician-integration capability before close.

**Three supports:**

1. At $850M, the deal works only under best-case integration assumptions; counter-offer reflects realistic execution.
2. Retention-bonus mechanism aligns deal structure with the actual risk (physician departure).
3. Clinical affiliation captures most of the strategic referral logic without the integration risk + capital lock-up.

**One risk:** the seller may have other interested buyers willing to pay $850M; walking away means losing strategic positioning. Mitigate by signaling willingness to come back at higher price if competitor offers fall through.

## What judges look for

- Did you apply the **M&A triad (strategic / valuation / integration)** as the structural framework?
- Did you size the **physician-retention risk** as the dominant integration concern?
- Did you challenge the **10x EBITDA multiple** under realistic integration assumptions?
- Did you propose **alternatives to outright acquisition** (clinical affiliation) rather than treating it as binary?
