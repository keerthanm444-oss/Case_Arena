## The situation

The "specialty vs commodity" portfolio question has been a defining theme in industrial chemicals for 30 years. The simple story — specialty is high-margin growth, commodity is dying — is often oversimplified. Commodity chemicals provide scale, integration benefits, and customer-relationship leverage that pure-specialty companies lack.

The question is whether Altruum's specific commodity business serves a strategic purpose or genuinely should be divested.

Three branches:

1. **Standalone economics** — what's the commodity business worth as a separate entity? Industry multiples, growth outlook, capital intensity.
2. **Integration value** — does the commodity business feed specialty? Vertical integration may justify keeping low-margin operations.
3. **Strategic option value** — does owning commodity capacity preserve flexibility (capacity for new specialty products) or customer relationships?

## Key analysis

The diagnostic test: **is the commodity business value-additive in the integrated portfolio, or is it just a drag?**

Three patterns commonly emerge:

**Pattern A — pure portfolio drag:** Commodity doesn't feed specialty; customers don't overlap; shared cost reduction is minimal. **Exit makes sense.**

**Pattern B — strategic integration:** Commodity output is the feedstock for 30%+ of specialty production. Exit would require buying inputs externally at higher cost or quality risk. **Keep.**

**Pattern C — customer leverage:** Same customers buy both. Specialty pricing power partly reflects "we make you all your chemicals" relationship. **Mixed — depends on how strong the customer lock-in really is.**

For most diversified chemical companies, the pattern is A for some commodity sub-segments and B/C for others. The right answer is rarely "exit all commodity" — it's "exit Pattern A commodity, retain Pattern B/C."

## Recommendation skeleton

**Recommendation:** **Selective commodity divestment.** Identify which commodity product lines fit Pattern A — likely 30-40% of commodity revenue. Divest these over 24-36 months (sale at 4-5x EBITDA). Retain Pattern B/C commodity lines. Reinvest proceeds in specialty growth + R&D. Target portfolio: 60% specialty / 40% commodity (integrated) by year 4.

**Three supports:**

1. Selective divestment captures most of the strategic benefit (focus, capital reallocation) without sacrificing integration value.
2. The 30-40% of commodity that's pure portfolio drag is the right size for divestment — large enough to matter, manageable to execute.
3. Retained commodity provides feedstock + customer-relationship advantages that pure-specialty competitors don't have.

**One risk:** sale prices for commodity chemicals are at cyclical lows. Mitigate by phasing divestments across the cycle rather than rushing to sell at once.

## What judges look for

- Did you reject **blanket commodity exit** for a more nuanced selective approach?
- Did you identify **integration value** as a distinct consideration from standalone economics?
- Did you classify commodity products into **multiple sub-segments** based on strategic role?
- Did you propose a **realistic 24-36 month divestment timeline** rather than treating exit as immediate?
