## The situation

Book publishing has navigated multiple format transitions over 20 years — ebook saturation, audiobook growth, subscription distribution models. Each transition has rewarded publishers who invested early in production + distribution capability. The current transition (audiobook scaling, AI narration emerging) is similar.

The strategic question: own more of the audio value chain (production, narration, even direct-to-consumer distribution) or continue licensing audio rights to platforms?

## Key analysis

Format economics:

| Format | Wholesale revenue per book | Production cost | Margin |
|--------|----------------------------|-----------------|--------|
| Hardcover print | $15 | $2-3 | High |
| Paperback | $7-10 | $1-2 | Medium-high |
| Ebook | $6-9 | $0.5 | High |
| Audiobook (Audible single sale) | $10-12 | $5-15 (narrator + studio) | Variable |
| Audiobook (Audible subscription stream) | $1-3 per listen | Same | Lower |

The non-obvious finding: **audiobook margin is much more variable than print or ebook**, depending on production cost + distribution model. A title that gets significant subscription streaming may produce lower per-unit revenue than expected.

For Arcadia specifically: aggressive audio investment is justified by growth — but production capability should be built carefully. Owning a small audio studio with 3-5 staff narrators for top-tier titles + freelance contracts for the rest is the realistic structure.

## Recommendation skeleton

**Recommendation:** **Audio-focused investment with platform diversification.** Track 1: build internal audio production capability — $20M investment in studio + staff narrator contracts; produce 40-50% of audiobooks internally within 24 months. Track 2: diversify distribution beyond Audible — relationships with Spotify, Apple, Libro.fm to reduce platform concentration. Track 3: maintain ebook + print optimization. Target audio revenue mix: 25% of total within 4 years.

**Three supports:**

1. Internal audio production captures margin currently going to outside producers.
2. Distribution diversification reduces dependency on dominant platform (Audible).
3. Growth in audio offsets continued print decline, supporting overall revenue.

**One risk:** Audible may respond to platform diversification with worse terms. Mitigate by maintaining strong relationship + ensuring Audible-exclusive titles when terms warrant.

## What judges look for

- Did you analyze **format-specific economics** rather than treating books uniformly?
- Did you size **audio production as variable margin** depending on distribution?
