## The situation

Tech M&A for AI talent has been one of the most active markets of the past 3 years. Acqui-hires of 20-50 engineer teams routinely close at $5-10M per engineer ($150-500M total), pricing that's clearly not justified by current revenue but is justified by **the cost + risk of building equivalent capability organically.**

The framing question: is Arcadia paying $180M for capability (~30 engineers at $6M each), for product, or for both? Each rationale supports a different deal structure.

Three branches — must all be yes:

1. **Strategic fit** — does the AI capability + product solve a clear strategic problem for Arcadia? Or is this acquiring trendy capability without a use case?
2. **Valuation** — is $180M defensible? Per-engineer: $6M (consistent with high-end acqui-hire). Per ARR: 60x (extreme by revenue standards). Per strategic-value (the capability we can't easily build): depends on Arcadia's roadmap.
3. **Integration risk** — can Arcadia retain the talent post-acquisition? Top-tier AI engineers have many options; the equity package + autonomy commitments matter.

## Key analysis

### Strategic fit

If Arcadia's roadmap explicitly includes AI features that this team's expertise unlocks (e.g., a specific product line that needs the talent the startup has), strategic fit is high. If it's "we need AI capability generally," strategic fit is much weaker — generic AI talent can be hired more cheaply.

### Valuation

Working through the three frames:

- **Acqui-hire frame:** $6M per engineer is at the high end of current market. Justifiable if the engineers are specifically critical talent (PhD-level, with rare skill specialization).
- **Revenue multiple frame:** 60x ARR is extreme. If the product itself is the rationale, the startup needs to have a path to $30M+ ARR for this to make sense.
- **Strategic value frame:** What would it cost Arcadia to build equivalent capability organically? Probably $80-150M over 3 years in recruiting + salaries + ramp time + roadmap delay. The acquisition saves 18-24 months.

The 3 frames give roughly the same answer: **$140-180M is the defensible range**, with the upper bound depending on how critical the capability is.

### Integration risk

The historical AI acqui-hire pattern: 30-50% of acquired engineers leave within 24 months. Mitigation requires retention packages (vested equity over 4 years), genuine autonomy (the acquired team continues as a distinct unit), and a real product mandate (not absorbed into existing teams).

## Recommendation skeleton

**Recommendation:** Acquire at $165M with structured retention. Allocate ~$30M of the price to acquired-engineer retention packages (vested over 4 years, double-trigger acceleration). Keep the acquired team as a distinct AI Studio with independent product mandate for 24 months. Specific KPIs: 80%+ engineer retention at month 18; integration of AI features into 2 Arcadia products by month 12.

**Three supports:**

1. The strategic value frame supports $140-180M; $165M offers some negotiating margin while staying within the defensible range.
2. Retention package addresses the largest integration risk (engineer departure) directly via deal structure.
3. AI Studio autonomy + specific KPIs gates outcome — if the team underperforms, Arcadia hasn't committed to absorbing them into the main org.

**One risk:** the AI talent market may correct sharply, making $165M look expensive in 12 months. Mitigate by accepting that capability + speed-to-market are the value, not asset preservation; the acquisition pays for itself even if standalone valuations drop.

## What judges look for

- Did you analyze the **three valuation frames** (per-engineer, per-ARR, strategic value) rather than picking one?
- Did you identify **engineer retention as the dominant integration risk** for AI acquisitions?
- Did you propose **retention structures + autonomy commitments** as deal-design elements?
- Did you specify **integration KPIs** that gate further investment, rather than open-ended commitment?
