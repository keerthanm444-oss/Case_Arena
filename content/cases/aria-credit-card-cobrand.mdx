## The situation

Co-brand credit card partnerships are some of the largest and most strategically loaded deals in consumer financial services. The economics involve interlocking flows: cardholder spending (revenue), interchange (revenue), miles cost (expense), airline-specific bounties (expense), funding costs, fraud, charge-offs. Small economics changes compound into hundreds of millions over a 10-year contract.

The renewal negotiation is high-stakes: walking away means losing the portfolio (cardholders typically migrate to whoever wins the contract); renewing at unsustainable economics means a decade of low profitability.

Three branches:

1. **Portfolio unit economics** — revenue per cardholder, miles cost per cardholder, net margin trajectory at current vs proposed terms.
2. **Competitive dynamics** — what are competing banks likely to bid? If a competitor is willing to pay materially more, can Aria match while remaining profitable?
3. **Walk-away alternative** — what's our position if we lose the contract? Cardholder retention, alternative product offerings, brand impact.

## Key analysis

Working through the economics:

**Current state:**
- 8M cardholders × $50 net margin/year = $400M revenue, ~$48M net profit
- Per cardholder economics: $80 interchange + $40 interest + $30 fees − $60 miles cost − $20 marketing − $20 other = $50 net

**Proposed renewal terms (airline's ask):**
- Per cardholder miles cost: $60 → $85 (higher mile payment)
- Per cardholder bounty: $20 → $35 (higher acquisition payment)
- Net margin: $50 → $20 per cardholder = $160M revenue, ~$10M profit

The new economics are barely break-even. **The airline is asking for more than the portfolio can profitably support** — unless cardholder spending volumes increase enough to offset, which would require either higher per-card spend or significant cardholder growth.

The non-obvious move: **counter with a deal structure that ties payments to performance.** Instead of fixed miles cost + bounty, propose variable structure where higher cardholder spend or higher engagement metrics earn higher airline payments. This aligns incentives — both parties benefit from a more engaged portfolio.

If the airline rejects performance-tied structure, the renewal is uneconomic. Walking away is rational despite the cardholder loss.

## Recommendation skeleton

**Recommendation:** **Counter with performance-tied economics.** Propose: lower fixed payments but variable bonuses tied to portfolio spend volume + new card additions. Offer $300-400M in committed marketing investment over the contract life. If airline refuses and demands fixed escalation, walk away — better to lose $50M annual profit than commit to a decade of $10M annual profit. Prepare a "Aria-branded travel rewards" alternative product to retain a share of the cardholder base.

**Three supports:**

1. Fixed-economics renewal at airline's ask is unprofitable; renewal only makes sense with variable structure.
2. Performance-tied economics protects Aria while still upside-aligning with airline goals.
3. Standalone product alternative provides a meaningful fallback (typically retains 25-35% of cardholders).

**One risk:** the airline may have a strong competing bid from Chase or Amex; our counter may be moot. Mitigate by preserving optionality — initiate the standalone product build before renewal closes, so we're not starting from scratch if we walk.

## What judges look for

- Did you build **per-cardholder unit economics** to assess the renewal terms?
- Did you propose **performance-tied structure** rather than just countering on price?
- Did you accept **walking away** as a rational option despite the portfolio loss?
- Did you prepare **a standalone alternative** to mitigate the walk-away downside?
