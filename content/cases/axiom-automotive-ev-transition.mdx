## The situation

Industrial transitions across product technologies (ICE → EV, mechanical → electronic) are among the most consequential decisions in manufacturing. The historical record is mixed — some incumbents (Bosch, Continental) transitioned across multiple technology shifts; others (Kodak, Nokia) lost their core business while failing in new markets.

Two failure patterns:

1. **Overcommit to the new technology before the old can fund it** — running out of cash mid-transition.
2. **Refuse to invest, banking on a slower transition than actually occurs** — missing the window.

The right strategy finds a path between these extremes.

Three branches:

1. **ICE business outlook** — how fast does ICE decline by geography? Where does it remain a cash engine 10-20 years out?
2. **EV component landscape** — which EV components have favorable industry structure? Battery cells = capital-intensive + commoditizing; thermal management + motor electronics = better positioned.
3. **Our right to win** — which EV components map to capabilities we already have (precision machining, customer relationships, materials science)?

## Key analysis

The diagnostic question: **which EV components could we credibly win in?**

A typical Tier-2 ICE supplier might map capabilities like this:

| Existing capability | Maps to EV component? | Realistic position |
|---------------------|-----------------------|---------------------|
| Precision machining | Motor housings, gear systems | Strong |
| Heat management (exhaust) | Battery thermal management | Strong |
| Electronic control units | Battery management systems | Medium |
| Materials science (high-temp) | Battery cells, thermal interfaces | Weak |
| Hydraulics + fluid systems | EV cooling systems | Strong |

So Axiom likely has a defensible position in **2-3 specific EV component categories**, not "EVs" broadly. Trying to win the entire EV supply chain is impossible; winning in 2-3 specific categories is realistic.

The non-obvious move: **the ICE business remains a cash engine for 10-15 years in many markets** (Latin America, Africa, parts of Asia). Aggressive ICE consolidation funds EV investment without external capital.

## Recommendation skeleton

**Recommendation:** **Dual-track strategy.** Consolidate the ICE business — close 30-40% of capacity over 5 years, harvest cash ($500M+ annually for the next decade). Invest in 2-3 specific EV component categories where Axiom has capability advantage (thermal management, motor housings, fluid systems). Target: 30%+ revenue from EV by year 8.

**Three supports:**

1. ICE consolidation generates the cash to fund EV investment without debt or dilution.
2. Focused EV bets in 2-3 capability-adjacent categories are realistic and defensible.
3. 8-year horizon gives time to develop EV while ICE cash flow remains predictable.

**One risk:** key engineering talent migrates to pure-EV companies during the transition. Mitigate with explicit retention packages for top engineers + a clear "this is a growth company" narrative anchored in EV investments.

## What judges look for

- Did you reject **"all in on EV" or "wait it out"** in favor of dual-track?
- Did you classify ICE business by geography rather than treating it as uniformly declining?
- Did you identify **specific EV component categories** where Axiom can win rather than treating EV as one bet?
- Did you size the **ICE cash engine as funding source** for the transition?
