## The situation

Biotech R&D portfolio decisions are among the highest-stakes capital allocation problems in any industry. Wrong cuts can kill the company; wrong holds bankrupt it. The discipline is **risk-adjusted NPV per dollar invested**, with explicit recognition that early-stage programs have higher upside variance but lower current capital absorption.

The 30% mandate is forcing prioritization that's been overdue.

## Structuring

For each of the 8 programs, calculate:

1. **Risk-adjusted NPV (rNPV)** = peak sales × PTRS at current phase − projected R&D cost to approval
2. **Capital efficiency** = rNPV ÷ R&D dollars required to next major milestone
3. **Strategic option value** = ability to outlicense or partner if kept alive
4. **Termination cost** = severance, write-offs, partnership wind-down

The 3 to kill aren't necessarily the lowest-rNPV programs — they're the lowest **rNPV per dollar of remaining R&D**.

## Key analysis

A typical pattern across 8 biotech R&D programs:

| Program | Phase | PTRS | Peak sales ($B) | rNPV ($B) | Capital to next milestone ($M) |
|---------|-------|------|-----------------|-----------|-------------------------------|
| A | III | 65% | 2.5 | 1.0 | 80 |
| B | III | 50% | 1.8 | 0.5 | 100 |
| C | II | 30% | 3.0 | 0.4 | 60 |
| D | II | 25% | 1.0 | 0.05 | 70 |
| E | I | 15% | 4.0 | 0.3 | 30 |
| F | I | 15% | 2.0 | 0.1 | 25 |
| G | Preclinical | 8% | 5.0 | 0.15 | 15 |
| H | Preclinical | 8% | 1.0 | -0.05 | 20 |

The naive cut: kill the 3 lowest rNPV (D, H, F).

The right cut considers capital efficiency: D (low rNPV, high capital), H (negative rNPV, low capital), and probably B (Phase III but high remaining cost relative to upside).

Killing B is counterintuitive — it's already in Phase III. But if it costs $100M to complete and only adds $500M risk-adjusted, the capital efficiency is worse than letting cheaper Phase I/II programs continue. The "save the late-stage programs" instinct often misallocates capital.

## Recommendation skeleton

**Recommendation:** Kill D and H (low rNPV per dollar). Out-license B to a larger pharma partner — its Phase III data is valuable to someone with deeper pockets even if it's not the right investment for us. Use the freed capital to accelerate A (highest rNPV) + maintain C and E (high upside variance). F and G continue at low burn but with explicit milestone-based gates.

**Three supports:**

1. Capital efficiency, not absolute rNPV, drives the right cut decisions — late-stage programs can be expensive winners that don't pay back.
2. Out-licensing B preserves option value while removing the cash burn.
3. The freed capital lets us accelerate the highest-rNPV program (A) rather than just save cash.

**One risk:** out-licensing in current biotech-market conditions may not yield a deal at acceptable economics. Mitigate by setting a 6-month timeline for the out-license process; if no deal closes, B continues internally but with a stop-loss budget.

## What judges look for

- Did you compute **rNPV per dollar of remaining R&D**, not absolute rNPV?
- Did you consider **out-licensing as an alternative to killing**? Most candidates only see kill/keep.
- Did you recognize that **the late-stage program can be the wrong investment** even if it's closer to approval?
- Did you propose **explicit gates** (out-license deadline, milestone-based budgets) rather than vague commitments?
