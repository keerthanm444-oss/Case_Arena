## The situation

Medical-device market entry into Europe combines three real complexities: regulatory (CE marking under MDR is meaningfully stricter than the prior MDD), reimbursement (decided country-by-country), and distribution (hospital sales is relationship-driven and country-specific).

The CEO's instinct to enter is reasonable — Europe is the second-largest medical-device market globally — but the entry mode and timing matter enormously.

## Key analysis

The non-obvious move: **EU entry should be sequenced by country, not done as a single launch.**

Country prioritization in most medical-device EU entries follows roughly this order:

1. **Germany** — largest market, fastest reimbursement (via DRG system), most receptive to clinical evidence
2. **UK** — English-speaking, NHS centralized procurement (slow but high-volume)
3. **France, Italy, Spain, Netherlands** — country-by-country, in roughly that priority order

Skipping the country-by-country sequencing and trying "EU launch" simultaneously usually fails: the reimbursement processes are different, the clinical-evidence requirements differ, and a partner that's effective in Germany may have no France presence.

Two timing considerations:

- **CE marking under MDR** typically takes 18-30 months from submission; significantly longer than US FDA 510(k) pathway. Budget at least 24 months from kickoff to first EU revenue.
- **Reimbursement decisions** add another 6-18 months per country after CE.

So the realistic horizon from "decide to enter" to "first meaningful EU revenue" is 30-48 months. The CEO needs to know this before committing.

## Recommendation skeleton

**Recommendation:** Enter Europe via a phased, country-sequenced approach starting with Germany. Submit for CE marking in Q1 2026. Build a 6-person Germany-focused team (medical affairs + sales). Re-evaluate at month 24 (CE issued?) and month 36 (Germany revenue trajectory) before committing to subsequent countries.

**Three supports:**

1. Country-sequenced entry limits capital at risk while building EU regulatory + commercial experience.
2. Germany-first maximizes the probability of a clean launch — best market structure + reimbursement clarity.
3. Phasing tied to specific milestones (CE, German DRG inclusion, year-1 revenue) creates explicit decision points rather than vague "review."

**One risk:** EU MDR regulatory timeline slippage delays revenue 12+ months. Mitigate by parallel-tracking the country-specific commercial work during the CE process so launch is ready when approval comes.

## What judges look for

- Did you reject "EU launch" as a single decision and sequence by country?
- Did you size the **regulatory timeline** (24+ months for CE under MDR) as a critical constraint?
- Did you specify **Germany-first** with a real rationale, not just "biggest market"?
- Did you propose **phased commitment** tied to specific milestones rather than a binary enter/don't-enter?
