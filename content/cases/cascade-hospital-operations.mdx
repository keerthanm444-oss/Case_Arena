## The situation

ED throughput is one of the most-studied healthcare operations problems. The pattern Cascade is describing — high utilization, extended LOS, negative margin contribution — usually reflects a flow problem, not an ED-capacity problem.

The diagnostic question: **where do patients spend their time in the ED?** A typical breakdown:

- **Triage to provider:** 30-90 minutes (provider availability)
- **Provider workup:** 60-120 minutes (orders, results, imaging)
- **Disposition decision:** 0-30 minutes
- **Wait for inpatient bed:** 60-600+ minutes (admitted patients waiting for inpatient capacity)

The last bucket — admitted patients **boarding** in the ED waiting for inpatient beds — is most often the dominant LOS driver. ED beds full of admitted patients = no capacity for new patients = utilization stays at 95%+ regardless of how fast the ED itself works.

## Structuring

Profitability framework adapted for the operational lens — the "cost" side is opportunity cost from constrained throughput:

Three branches:

1. **ED-internal flow** — provider staffing, triage process, order set efficiency, fast-track for low-acuity.
2. **Boarding (downstream bottleneck)** — inpatient discharge timing, weekend discharge volumes, capacity allocation.
3. **Inflow management** — Are some ED visits avoidable (non-emergent conditions that could be primary care)? Are payer mix or community-care alternatives in play?

## Key analysis

The diagnostic move that resolves most cases like this: **measure the share of ED LOS attributable to boarding.** If >40% of admitted patients board for 2+ hours, the bottleneck is downstream — fixing the ED itself won't help.

A typical Cascade-like pattern:

- 18-22% of ED visits result in admission
- 50-60% of those admissions board for 4+ hours
- Average boarding patient occupies an ED bed that would otherwise serve 1.5-2 new visits

The math: solving boarding can free up 30-40% of ED bed-hours, which translates directly to throughput improvement without adding any physical capacity.

The structural fix for boarding is **inpatient discharge timing** — most hospitals discharge inpatients between 11am-5pm. If discharges shifted earlier (e.g. by 10am), inpatient beds open up before the ED's peak admission hours (afternoon/evening), and boarding drops materially.

## Recommendation skeleton

**Recommendation:** Implement an **early-discharge initiative** for inpatient units (target: 50% of discharges by 11am, up from typical 20%). Pair with an **ED fast-track** for ESI level 4-5 visits. Re-measure after 90 days; if boarding shares haven't dropped, escalate to inpatient capacity expansion.

**Three supports:**

1. Boarding is the largest single LOS driver; addressing it has the highest leverage.
2. Early-discharge is operationally low-cost (process change + dashboard) compared to capacity expansion (build/buy).
3. Fast-track addresses the secondary lever — lower-acuity patients can move through faster, opening more ED bed-hours.

**One risk:** early-discharge requires coordination with discharge planning, transport, and (often) family logistics. Mitigate by piloting on 2 medical units before system-wide rollout.

## What judges look for

- Did you identify **boarding** as the likely root cause, rather than ED-internal flow?
- Did you separate the **operational fix** (early discharge) from the **structural fix** (capacity)?
- Did you propose a measurement protocol (re-measure after 90 days) rather than committing to capital before validating?
- Did you call out the **payer-mix dependency** — ED throughput improvements have very different economic value depending on whether new visits are commercially insured?
