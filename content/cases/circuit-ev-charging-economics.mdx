## The situation

EV charging network economics have been one of the most-discussed infrastructure investment categories of the past 5 years. The thesis is straightforward: EVs need charging, install before demand. The reality is harder: utilization ramps slowly, fixed costs are high, and many networks lose money even as EV adoption accelerates.

The break-even math: a 6-stall DC fast-charging station costs ~$1.5-3M to build, has $100-300K annual fixed costs, requires 20-25% utilization to break even on operations + cover depreciation.

## Key analysis

A typical utilization distribution across a 2,000-station network:

| Utilization tier | Station count | Annual P&L |
|------------------|---------------|------------|
| 25%+ (highway, dense urban) | 200-300 | Profitable |
| 15-25% (mid-tier urban) | 600-800 | Break-even to slight loss |
| <15% (suburban, low-traffic) | 900-1,100 | Loss-making |

The non-obvious finding: **building more stations makes the network problem worse, not better.** Average utilization across the network falls as low-utilization sites are added.

For Circuit specifically:
- The 200-300 high-utilization sites cover their share of corporate overhead + generate cash
- The middle tier breaks even, contributes some overhead absorption
- The bottom 900-1,100 sites destroy value — they lose money on operations AND tie up capital

The strategic answer is **selective consolidation** — close 200-400 worst-performing sites, redirect capital to fleet customer acquisition (predictable utilization), and grow new stations only in proven high-utilization geographies.

Fleet customer dynamics: a delivery fleet charging 50 vehicles daily generates 60-80% utilization at dedicated stations. Fleet customers also pay subscription fees, smoothing revenue.

## Recommendation skeleton

**Recommendation:** **Network consolidation + fleet pivot.** Close 250-300 lowest-utilization stations over 24 months (saves $40-60M annual loss). Invest $80M in fleet customer acquisition + dedicated fleet charging hubs. Pause new public retail station construction except in proven high-utilization geographies. Target: network-wide profitability by year 3.

**Three supports:**

1. Closing loss-making sites recovers capital + stops the bleeding while preserving the profitable core.
2. Fleet customers offer higher + more predictable utilization than retail public charging.
3. Pausing speculative expansion focuses capital on validated economics.

**One risk:** competitors continue aggressive build-out, locking up future high-traffic sites. Mitigate by maintaining site-acquisition pipeline (options, leases) without immediate buildout — be ready to construct when utilization projections justify.
