## The situation

Beverage SKU rationalization is a perennial CPG problem. The classic 80/20 dynamic is amplified — top 5-10 SKUs usually drive 60-70% of revenue while the long tail of 40-50 SKUs drives <15%. The challenge: long-tail SKUs aren't pure waste. They serve specific channels, satisfy retailer assortment expectations, or block competitive shelf placements.

## Structuring

For each SKU, classify on three dimensions:

1. **Revenue contribution** — top decile, mid, long tail
2. **Strategic role** — flagship, channel-specific (e.g., foodservice), defensive (blocks competitor), legacy
3. **Operational cost** — manufacturing complexity, distribution drag, shelf-space cost

## Key analysis

A typical 60-SKU breakdown:

| Tier | SKU count | Revenue share | Action |
|------|-----------|---------------|--------|
| Top (>$50M) | 8-10 | 60-70% | Invest |
| Mid ($10-50M) | 15-20 | 20-25% | Keep, optimize |
| Long tail (<$10M) | 30-35 | 8-12% | **Rationalize selectively** |

For the long tail, three sub-categories:

- **Channel-specific** (e.g., 12oz can only sold in convenience): keep if channel contribution is meaningful
- **Defensive** (blocks a competitor SKU): keep if competitor threat is real
- **Pure legacy** (declining, no channel role, no competitive function): **kill**

For a 60-SKU portfolio, typically 15-20 SKUs are pure legacy. Killing these recovers manufacturing complexity savings (5-10% on overhead allocation), simplifies sales conversations, and frees shelf-space capital for innovation.

The non-obvious move: **brand rationalization should follow SKU rationalization, not lead it.** Brands with only 1-2 remaining SKUs after rationalization become natural divestment or sunset candidates.

## Recommendation skeleton

**Recommendation:** **Two-phase rationalization over 18 months.** Phase 1 (6 months): kill 18 pure-legacy SKUs; redirect freed shelf space + manufacturing capacity to top 10 SKUs. Phase 2 (12 months): sunset or divest 3-4 brands that drop to <2 SKUs each. Target end-state: 9 brands, 38 SKUs, with top 8 SKUs driving 70% of revenue.

**Three supports:**

1. SKU rationalization is reversible and low-risk; brand rationalization (politically harder) only happens where math forces it.
2. Phasing avoids retailer disruption — gradual SKU reduction is absorbed more easily than wholesale cuts.
3. Manufacturing complexity savings compound — each SKU eliminated unlocks ~$0.5-1M in annual overhead efficiency.

**One risk:** retailers may interpret aggressive SKU cuts as supplier-weakness signal and reduce shelf allocation. Mitigate by simultaneously launching 2-3 high-potential innovation SKUs to replace some legacy slots, maintaining total SKU count for retail relations while improving portfolio mix.

## What judges look for

- Did you classify SKUs along **multiple dimensions** (revenue, strategic role, cost)?
- Did you identify **defensive + channel-specific** as legitimate reasons to keep low-revenue SKUs?
- Did you propose **brand rationalization following SKU**, not as a separate decision?
- Did you address **retailer perception** of SKU reduction?
