## The situation

Cobblestone sits in the squeezed middle of footwear retail. The $40 tier eats share via fast-fashion-style refresh cycles; the $200 tier wins on aspiration + heritage marketing. Cobblestone has neither edge — its $80-120 product is functionally fine but emotionally generic.

The CEO's instinct is to "go premium". That's a major strategic move: brand re-architecture, product portfolio changes, channel exit, marketing investment. Before recommending, the question to answer is whether Cobblestone has the **right to win** in the $150-200 tier.

- **Customer** — is the premium consumer's purchase decision driven by attributes Cobblestone has (quality, craftsmanship) or doesn't (heritage, status symbol)?
- **Competition** — what is the existing $150-200 player set, and what defines their advantages? Are they vulnerable on any axis Cobblestone could attack?
- **Company** — what assets does Cobblestone have that translate to a $150+ tier? Manufacturing capability typically translates; brand equity rarely does.

## Key analysis

Two numbers usually decide this case:

**Number 1 — premium-tier gross margin:** $150-200 footwear typically runs 55-65% GM at retail<Citation publisher="Case in Point" title="Case in Point — Complete Case Interview Preparation" authors={['Cosentino, Marc']} year={2023} needsVerification /> vs. 40-45% at Cobblestone's current tier. The margin uplift funds the DTC operations, marketing, and slower turnover required at the premium tier.

**Number 2 — DTC customer-acquisition cost:** Premium footwear DTC CAC is structurally high because the buyer wants tactile experience first. CAC payback periods of 18-24 months are common. Cobblestone needs to model whether its existing customer base will transition (most won't) and whether new-customer acquisition economics work.

The honest read: Cobblestone has manufacturing + product-quality assets that map to premium, but lacks brand heritage and DTC operational depth. The two missing pieces take 3-5 years and significant investment to build.

## Recommendation skeleton

**Recommendation:** Do **not** reposition the entire brand. Instead, launch a premium sub-line at $150-180 under a new sub-brand, maintain the core $80-120 lineup, and pilot DTC for the sub-line only. Re-evaluate at 24 months with explicit go/no-go criteria.

**Three supports:**

1. A binary "reposition or not" is the wrong frame — the gradient between "stay" and "pivot fully" includes a sub-brand pilot that preserves optionality.
2. The premium sub-line lets Cobblestone build DTC capability without betting the wholesale business.
3. If the sub-line works, Cobblestone can migrate the brand toward premium over 5 years. If it fails, the core business is intact.

**One risk:** the sub-line dilutes management focus + capital from the core. Mitigate by capping sub-line investment at <15% of total marketing spend in years 1-2.

## What judges look for

- Did you separate the **brand repositioning** decision from the **channel strategy** decision? They're related but not identical.
- Did you propose a **staged commitment** (sub-brand) rather than a binary pivot?
- Did you size **DTC CAC + payback** as a critical input rather than waving at it?
- Did you call out the brand-equity gap honestly? Premium tier rewards heritage Cobblestone doesn't have.
