## The situation

Federal procurement reform is a recurring theme of administration changes. Typical savings targets — 10-15% of addressable spend — are achievable through a combination of demand management, vendor consolidation, and process automation. The barriers are political (small-business mandates, congressional district distribution) and operational (decentralized contracting authority, manual processes).

For a $14B procurement portfolio, $2B (14%) is at the upper end of achievable savings — requires aggressive execution across multiple levers.

Three branches:

1. **Demand management** — do we need to buy as much? Service consolidation, contract right-sizing, requirements consolidation across sub-agencies.
2. **Price/terms** — vendor consolidation, competitive sourcing, contract renegotiation, terms standardization.
3. **Process efficiency** — automation, e-procurement, category management.

## Key analysis

Typical procurement savings decomposition:

| Lever | Savings range | Time to realize |
|-------|---------------|-----------------|
| Demand management | 4-7% of spend | 12-24 months |
| Vendor consolidation | 3-5% | 18-36 months |
| Competitive sourcing on long-tail | 2-4% | 12-18 months |
| Process automation | 1-2% | 24-36 months |
| Contract renegotiation | 2-4% | 6-18 months |

Total potential: 12-22% of spend = $1.7-3.1B savings. The $2B target sits in the realistic middle.

The non-obvious finding: **demand management is the largest single lever but also the hardest politically.** Reducing demand requires saying "we don't need this" — which conflicts with sub-agency budget protection instincts.

For the agency specifically:
- IT consulting ($3B): vendor consolidation + competitive sourcing has 15-20% savings potential
- Professional services ($4B): demand management + competitive sourcing
- Facilities ($3B): space consolidation, energy efficiency, lease renegotiation
- Other ($4B): category management

## Recommendation skeleton

**Recommendation:** **Three-track $2B savings program over 36 months.** Track 1: demand management — consolidate IT + consulting requirements across sub-agencies ($600-800M savings). Track 2: vendor + sourcing optimization — competitive sourcing on top 50 categories ($600-800M). Track 3: process + automation — deploy e-procurement, category management ($300-500M, longer payback). Centralize category management for top 20 categories at agency level.

**Three supports:**

1. Demand management is the largest lever — must be tackled despite political difficulty.
2. Centralized category management captures cross-sub-agency consolidation savings.
3. Process automation provides sustainable savings beyond initial program.

**One risk:** sub-agency leadership resists centralization. Mitigate with explicit Secretary mandate + transparent savings tracking + reinvestment of savings into mission-priority areas (creates win-win optics).

## What judges look for

- Did you identify **demand management** as the largest single lever?
- Did you propose **category centralization** to capture cross-sub-agency consolidation?
- Did you size **each lever's savings potential** explicitly?
- Did you address **political constraints** on procurement reform?
