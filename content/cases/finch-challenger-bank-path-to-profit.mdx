## The situation

Challenger banks have a well-documented monetization problem. The product (free, mobile-first banking) acquires users cheaply but monetizes poorly. Interchange revenue per user is $20-50/year; subscription revenue (where it exists) is $50-200/year for the small share who pay; lending and overdraft revenue varies enormously by user mix and credit policy.

The honest math: 12M users × $20-40 interchange ARPU = $240-480M revenue ceiling on the current model. Finch is at $80M — meaning either the monetization is much lower than ceiling, or a significant share of users aren't primary-account active.

Three branches:

1. **Revenue diagnosis** — current ARPU breakdown, user-mix analysis (primary vs secondary accounts), pricing power.
2. **Cost diagnosis** — where does the $180M annual loss come from? Marketing CAC, engineering headcount, compliance, fraud losses, banking-platform costs.
3. **Strategic options** — premium subscription, lending, BaaS pivot, cost cuts. Each has different time-to-impact and risk profile.

## Key analysis

The diagnostic question: **what share of 12M users are primary-account active?**

For most challenger banks:

- 20-30% are primary-account users (their main checking, salary deposits, daily transactions): ARPU $50-100
- 30-40% are secondary-account users (one card, low usage): ARPU $5-15
- 30-40% are dormant or near-dormant: ARPU near zero

If Finch's mix is 25% primary, 35% secondary, 40% dormant:

- 3M primary × $80 = $240M
- 4.2M secondary × $10 = $42M
- Total revenue potential at current monetization: ~$280M

The gap to current $80M suggests either (a) primary user count is much lower than estimated, or (b) monetization per primary user is well below industry norms.

The non-obvious move: **the path to profitability is primary-user growth, not new product launches.** Going from 3M primary to 5M primary at $80 ARPU adds $160M revenue — close to closing the loss.

Launching lending products is alluring but introduces credit risk that challenger banks struggle with. BaaS pivot is a different business that requires different team + capability.

## Recommendation skeleton

**Recommendation:** **Focus on primary-account conversion + selective monetization.** Action 1: invest in primary-account features (direct deposit incentives, bill pay, savings tools) targeting 50%+ primary-account conversion from current secondary users. Action 2: roll out a $9.99/month premium tier with concrete value (cashback, fee-free FX) for top 10% of users. Action 3: cut marketing spend on top-of-funnel acquisition by 40% — Finch has the users, monetization is the issue. Defer lending launch by 24 months until banking-core profitability is achieved.

**Three supports:**

1. Primary-account conversion is the highest-leverage lever with lowest execution risk.
2. Premium subscription captures incremental ARPU from already-engaged users at high incremental margin.
3. Marketing reduction directly cuts burn; the user-growth phase has done its job.

**One risk:** competitors continue acquiring new users at scale while Finch consolidates; market-share could shrink. Mitigate by maintaining moderate brand marketing (cut 40%, not 80%) — preserving brand strength without continuing the burn-rate problem.

## What judges look for

- Did you identify **primary-account share** as the diagnostic metric rather than total user count?
- Did you reject **new product launches** (lending, BaaS) as primary path to profitability?
- Did you size the **primary-user conversion math** to demonstrate path-to-profitability feasibility?
- Did you propose **specific cost cuts** (marketing) rather than vague "reduce burn"?
