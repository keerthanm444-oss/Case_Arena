## The situation

Usage-based pricing in B2B SaaS is one of the most-discussed pricing topics of the past decade. The success stories (Snowflake, Twilio, AWS) are real but specific: usage-based works when **usage tracks value** and the buyer's economics improve as usage grows.

For workflow-automation, the question is harder. Workflow executions don't always correlate with customer value — some customers run high-frequency, low-value workflows; others run low-frequency, high-value ones. Pricing by execution count would penalize the first group and underprice the second.

Three branches:

1. **Customer perspective** — does the customer's business value scale with workflow executions, or is the relationship non-linear?
2. **Competitive landscape** — what are similar workflow-automation tools pricing on? Is usage-based the standard in this category or an outlier?
3. **Company economics** — how does usage-based change revenue predictability, sales-team incentives, and gross margin under different customer behaviors?

## Key analysis

The diagnostic question for usage-based viability: **for a given customer, does increasing usage create proportional value?**

Three common patterns:

- **Linear value (usage works well):** Database queries → analytical insights → business decisions. More queries usually equals more value.
- **Stepped value (usage works imperfectly):** Marketing automation → email sends. The 10,000th email per month often delivers diminishing returns vs the first 1,000.
- **Inverse value (usage works poorly):** Workflow automation → process executions. A customer who runs more workflows might be solving more problems, but they might also just have a worse-designed process.

Workflow automation tends to sit in the third bucket. Charging per execution rewards the customer for **using your product less** — which creates a strange churn dynamic where loyal customers feel punished.

The honest analysis usually shows: pure usage-based pricing **is the wrong move for workflow automation**, but a **hybrid model** (seat-based base + usage overage above a threshold) captures the upside of expansion revenue without the downside of penalizing core use.<Citation publisher="McKinsey & Company" title="McKinsey Quarterly" />

## Recommendation skeleton

**Recommendation:** Do **not** move to pure usage-based pricing. Instead, introduce a **hybrid tier structure**: seat-based pricing for the first N workflows per seat per month (base tier), with usage-based overage above the threshold. Test this on new customers for 6 months before migrating existing customers.

**Three supports:**

1. Workflow executions don't reliably track customer value — pure usage-based punishes power users.
2. The hybrid model captures expansion revenue (high-usage customers pay more) without disrupting the existing predictable-revenue base.
3. Testing on new customers first limits exposure; existing customers can be migrated only when the new model is validated.

**One risk:** the hybrid model is harder to explain in sales conversations than either pure seat or pure usage. Mitigate by simplifying the pricing calculator + training the sales team on the most common customer-decision scenarios.

## What judges look for

- Did you challenge the "usage-based is universally better" framing rather than accepting it?
- Did you map **usage-to-value** carefully? Workflow automation is the case where this mapping breaks.
- Did you propose a **hybrid model** rather than treating it as binary?
- Did you design a **staged rollout** (new customers first) to limit downside?
