## The situation

Pipeline infrastructure investment is among the most capital-intensive decisions in energy. Pipelines have very long economic lives (30-50 years), highly fixed costs, and value-creation depends heavily on take-or-pay contract coverage. Underutilized pipelines are extremely expensive — fixed costs continue regardless of throughput.

The strategic question: is 60% contracted enough, or should we wait?

Three branches:

1. **Demand attractiveness** — long-term natural gas demand outlook, LNG export growth, regional production trajectory.
2. **Contract coverage** — quality and duration of existing take-or-pay commitments, willingness of additional shippers to commit.
3. **Risk profile** — regulatory delay, cost overrun, stranded asset risk if transition accelerates.

## Key analysis

The economics at 60% contracted capacity:

- Annual revenue from take-or-pay: $200-250M
- Annual fixed costs (depreciation + opex + interest): $250-300M
- Annual loss on partially-contracted basis: ~$50M

Even with spot-market revenue from the remaining 40% capacity (maybe $50-100M at typical fill rates), the project IRR is only 6-8% — below typical pipeline cost-of-capital (9-11%).

At 80% contracted:
- Annual take-or-pay revenue: $260-330M
- Same fixed costs
- Net annual cash flow: $50-100M positive
- Project IRR: 10-13%

The non-obvious move: **delay 12-18 months to secure additional contracts** rather than building now and hoping for spot market fill. Pipeline projects can't be unwound; getting the contract structure right before construction is far cheaper than fixing it after.

## Recommendation skeleton

**Recommendation:** **Delay construction 12-18 months to secure 80%+ contracted capacity.** Continue regulatory + engineering work to maintain timeline if contracts emerge. Negotiate aggressively with additional shippers (LNG exporters, producers) — offer rate discounts in exchange for long-term commitment. If 80% threshold isn't reached in 18 months, scale down pipeline to 300 miles (lower capex, smaller capacity targeting just the committed customers).

**Three supports:**

1. Pipeline economics don't work at 60% contracted — investing now creates value-destroying asset.
2. 12-18 month delay is cheap relative to lifetime cost of underutilized capacity.
3. Scale-down option (300 miles) preserves value if full project doesn't get contract coverage.

**One risk:** competitor builds first and captures the demand. Mitigate by maintaining permits + engineering work — Fortis remains positioned to move quickly if contracts materialize.

## What judges look for

- Did you size **economics at different contract coverages** rather than treating 60% as good enough?
- Did you propose **delay** as a strategic move rather than treating it as failure?
- Did you propose **scale-down option** as a fallback?
- Did you recognize **pipeline economics' high fixed-cost sensitivity**?
