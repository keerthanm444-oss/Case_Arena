## The situation

Manufacturing-network optimization is one of the most common strategic projects for diversified industrial companies. The math usually shows substantial savings (10-20% of operating cost) from consolidation, but execution is constrained by customer-proximity requirements, labor agreements, regulatory obligations, and strategic flexibility considerations.

The "close the low-utilization plants" instinct is usually wrong without further decomposition.

Three branches:

1. **Demand side** — where are customers geographically? What does the shipping-cost-to-revenue ratio look like by region?
2. **Supply side (plants)** — for each of the 28 plants, what's the utilization, cost position, product capability, and closure cost?
3. **Network design** — given customer locations + plant capabilities, what's the optimal network? Usually requires linear-programming style optimization, but the qualitative inputs (which plants are unique-capability) dominate.

## Key analysis

The diagnostic question: **classify the 28 plants on capability + cost position + location.**

A typical breakdown:

| Plant type | Count | Action |
|------------|-------|--------|
| Unique-capability, low-cost, customer-proximate | 4-6 | Keep + invest |
| Multi-purpose, mid-cost, customer-proximate | 8-10 | Keep, but optimize utilization |
| Multi-purpose, high-cost, customer-distant | 6-8 | **Candidates for closure** |
| Unique-capability, high-cost, customer-distant | 2-4 | Migrate capability to better-positioned plant before closure |
| Strategic-flexibility (excess capacity buffer) | 2-3 | Keep |

The non-obvious finding: closing the "candidates for closure" bucket alone (~30% of plants) can lift consolidated utilization from 64% to 80%+ without losing customer service, because demand transfers to retained plants in the same regions.

Closure economics:

- US/Mexico: $20-50M per plant (severance + facility costs)
- Western Europe: $60-150M per plant (regulated severance, often 1-3 years of process)
- Asia: $10-30M per plant (varies by country)

The net present value of closing 6-8 plants typically runs $400-800M positive over 5 years, despite the upfront closure costs.

## Recommendation skeleton

**Recommendation:** Close 6 plants over 36 months in two waves. Wave 1 (year 1): 3 US/Mexico plants where utilization <50% and customers can be served from nearby plants. Wave 2 (year 3): 3 EU plants requiring the longer regulated closure process. Invest savings in capacity expansion at 4 retained low-cost plants. Target: consolidated utilization 80%, operating margin 11% by year 4.

**Three supports:**

1. Two-wave sequencing matches the operational reality of closures — fast where feasible, longer timelines where regulated.
2. Capacity reinvestment in retained plants prevents capacity shortfall as demand transfers.
3. Specific plant selection criteria (utilization + customer proximity + closure cost) generates a defensible target list rather than a gut call.

**One risk:** customer commitments tied to specific plants may not transfer cleanly; some customers may require renegotiation. Mitigate by engaging top 10 customers on the transition plan in year 1, before any closure announcement.

## What judges look for

- Did you **classify plants into multiple sub-types** rather than closing on utilization alone?
- Did you size **closure costs by geography** as a material constraint?
- Did you propose **wave-based execution** matching the legal/regulatory realities?
- Did you address **customer impact** explicitly with a transition plan?
