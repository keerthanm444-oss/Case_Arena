## The situation

Offshore wind represents the next frontier of US renewable development. Federal lease auctions, state procurement targets (NY, NJ, MA, VA), and falling LCOE create a meaningful TAM. But offshore is operationally much more complex than onshore — different vessels, foundations, transmission, regulatory framework, and timelines.

For an onshore-only developer, offshore entry requires either substantial capability build or strategic partnership.

Three branches:

1. **Market attractiveness** — offshore TAM (50GW+ by 2030 in US), growth rate, PPA pricing differential vs onshore.
2. **Capability gap** — offshore-specific requirements (vessels, foundations, transmission engineering, marine permitting) that Galewind doesn't have.
3. **Entry mode** — go alone (high risk), JV with offshore developer (shares risk + accelerates capability), acquire offshore developer (most expensive).

## Key analysis

Comparing offshore vs onshore economics:

| Metric | Onshore | Offshore |
|--------|---------|----------|
| Capex per MW | $1.5M | $3-5M |
| Capacity factor | 35-45% | 45-55% |
| PPA price | $25-50/MWh | $80-180/MWh |
| Time to operation | 18-30 months | 6-10 years |
| Capital risk | Lower | Much higher |

The non-obvious finding: **offshore project economics can be attractive but the time horizon + capital risk + capability gap make solo entry imprudent for a first-timer.** Galewind would expose substantial capital with limited offshore-specific expertise.

The realistic options:

- **JV with established offshore developer** (e.g., Equinor, Ørsted): Galewind contributes capital + US permitting/community capability; partner contributes offshore engineering + project management. Shares risk, accelerates learning.
- **Acquire small offshore developer**: faster capability gain but high acquisition multiples.
- **Wait + observe**: defer 3-5 years until offshore market matures + costs decline.

## Recommendation skeleton

**Recommendation:** **Enter via JV with established offshore developer.** Target 30-40% stake in 1-2 offshore projects over 5 years. Galewind commits $400-800M of equity capital. Use these projects to build internal offshore capability (engineering, procurement, project management). After 2 successful JV projects, evaluate solo offshore development. Continue onshore growth in parallel.

**Three supports:**

1. JV captures economic upside while limiting capital risk + capability burden.
2. Established partner brings offshore-specific experience Galewind lacks.
3. Phased capability build positions for solo offshore development in 7-10 years if economics justify.

**One risk:** strong JV partners may demand majority interest or unfavorable terms. Mitigate by being patient — multiple developers (Equinor, Ørsted, Avangrid, Iberdrola) compete for US partnerships; we don't need to take first offer.

## What judges look for

- Did you reject **solo offshore entry** for capability + capital reasons?
- Did you propose **JV partnership** as the realistic entry mode?
- Did you size **offshore vs onshore economics differential** explicitly?
- Did you address **timeline differences** (6-10 years vs 18-30 months) as a strategic constraint?
