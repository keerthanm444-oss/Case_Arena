## The situation

Class-8 truck electrification is in early stages but accelerating. The economic case varies dramatically by route profile: regional routes returning to a fixed depot favor electric (charging at depot overnight); long-haul routes don't (range + charging infrastructure inadequate). State mandates (California ACT) and customer preferences (Amazon, Walmart demanding clean fleets) are creating pressure regardless of pure economics.

The right pace depends on route mix, capital flexibility, and competitive dynamics.

Three branches:

1. **Route profile analysis** — which routes are electric-compatible today? Regional with depot return is the green zone; long-haul is gray zone (waiting for infrastructure + battery tech).
2. **Total-cost-of-ownership** — purchase price + fuel + maintenance + driver downtime for charging vs diesel comparison.
3. **Strategic considerations** — customer demand, regulatory pressure, brand positioning.

## Key analysis

Electric vs diesel Class-8 economics for regional route:

| Metric | Diesel | Electric |
|--------|--------|----------|
| Purchase price | $170K | $450K |
| Fuel cost (per 100K miles) | $35K | $12K (depot electricity) |
| Maintenance (per 100K miles) | $20K | $12K |
| Charging infrastructure (allocated) | $0 | $30K |
| Federal tax credit | $0 | -$40K |
| Net 10-year cost (1M miles) | $720K | $710K |

Roughly break-even on TCO at current prices for regional routes — incentives + scale will tilt toward electric over time.

For long-haul, the math is much worse:
- Range issues (300-500 mile range vs daily 700-900 mile demand)
- Public charging infrastructure inadequate
- Driver downtime for 60-90 min charging breaks (compared to 10 min diesel refuel)

The non-obvious move: **electrify regional routes aggressively, defer long-haul until 2027-2030.** Regional fleet electrification is economically competitive today; long-haul economics + operations don't work yet.

## Recommendation skeleton

**Recommendation:** **Phased electrification matched to route profile.** Phase 1 (years 1-3): electrify 40-50% of regional fleet (~600-800 trucks) — strong economics, depot charging readily installed. Phase 2 (years 4-7): expand based on long-haul technology readiness; target 30% total fleet electric by 2030. Phase 3 (years 7-10): scale long-haul as technology + infrastructure matures. Total target: 40-50% electric by 2032.

**Three supports:**

1. Regional routes have favorable TCO + operations economics today.
2. Long-haul deferral avoids buying technology that won't operate effectively for years.
3. Phased approach builds operational + technical capability with manageable risk.

**One risk:** state mandates (California, more states following) may force faster transition than economics support. Mitigate by California-specific accelerated transition for routes operating in regulated states; maintain national fleet flexibility.

## What judges look for

- Did you separate **regional vs long-haul** as fundamentally different electrification cases?
- Did you size **TCO including charging infrastructure**?
- Did you propose **route-specific phasing** rather than uniform transition?
- Did you address **state regulatory variation** as a constraint?
