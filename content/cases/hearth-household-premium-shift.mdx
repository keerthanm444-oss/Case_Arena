## The situation

Mass-market household-goods erosion to retailer private-label has been one of the dominant trends in US consumer goods over 20 years. Retailers extracted scale-driven cost positions (Costco Kirkland, Walmart Great Value, Amazon brands) and produced credible quality at much lower prices. Branded CPG companies are forced to choose: defend at thin margins, retreat to premium, or attempt new positioning.

Three branches:

1. **Mass-market dynamics** — private-label share, growth rate, brand-margin sustainability, retailer leverage if we exit.
2. **Premium opportunity** — TAM size, growth rate, competitive density, our right to win.
3. **Operational interdependence** — do mass-market and premium share manufacturing, sales force, distribution? Full exit costs may be larger than they appear.

## Key analysis

The diagnostic test: **for mass-market specifically, what's the realistic 5-year margin trajectory?**

A typical pattern:

- Mass-market gross margin compressing from 35% to 25% as retailer leverage increases
- Mass-market unit volume declining 3-5% annually to private-label
- Premium gross margin stable at 50-55%, volume growing 5-8%

If mass-market crosses below cost-of-capital ROIC within 3-5 years, exit makes economic sense. But shared infrastructure means exit isn't free — losing volume scale at shared plants raises premium per-unit costs.

The non-obvious move: **selective mass-market presence rather than full exit.** Keep mass-market SKUs in product categories where shared manufacturing is critical, exit categories where mass-market is operationally separable. Premium investment focuses on adjacencies (premium-natural, professional-grade, specialty) that command true premium pricing.

## Recommendation skeleton

**Recommendation:** **Selective tier strategy.** Maintain mass-market in laundry (shared with premium plants) but exit dishwashing mass-market entirely (no manufacturing overlap, declining category). Invest $200M over 3 years in premium-natural + professional-grade adjacencies. Target: 60% revenue from premium tiers by year 5; mass-market scale-down rather than zero.

**Three supports:**

1. Manufacturing interdependence makes full mass-market exit operationally damaging — selective approach preserves scale benefits.
2. Premium investment in adjacencies has higher growth potential than defending existing premium tier.
3. Dish + laundry category dynamics differ; treating them uniformly underperforms category-specific strategies.

**One risk:** retailers may reduce premium-tier shelf allocation if Hearth exits mass-market category entirely. Mitigate by signaling continued category commitment via premium-tier investment + retailer-specific premium variants.

## What judges look for

- Did you reject **blanket exit** in favor of category-specific decisions?
- Did you identify **shared manufacturing** as a binding constraint on exit economics?
- Did you size **premium adjacencies** as the strategic growth bet?
- Did you address **retailer perception** of mass-market exit?
