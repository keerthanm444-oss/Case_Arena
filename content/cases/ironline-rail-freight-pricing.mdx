## The situation

US Class-I railroad pricing has evolved over the past 15 years toward precision scheduled railroading (PSR) — emphasizing asset productivity, network optimization, predictable scheduling. Volume-based multi-year contracts complement this model by giving railroads predictable demand to optimize against.

The tradeoff: railroad gives up pricing flexibility for shipper commitment to volume + multi-year duration.

Three branches:

1. **Commodity-level economics** — different bulk commodities have different bargaining power, volume predictability, and substitution options.
2. **Asset productivity impact** — volume predictability allows better crew + locomotive + track utilization.
3. **Contract structure** — fixed price, indexed price, volume commitments, take-or-pay provisions.

## Key analysis

Commodity-level analysis:

| Commodity | Volume volatility | Shipper alternatives | Best contract type |
|-----------|-------------------|---------------------|---------------------|
| Coal | High (declining) | Limited (rail-dependent) | Short-term, indexed |
| Grain | High (weather) | Some (truck) | Annual, weather-adjusted |
| Chemicals | Moderate | Limited (rail-required) | Multi-year, indexed |
| Intermodal | Moderate | Truck competition | Multi-year, market-rate |
| Auto/finished goods | Stable | Truck competition | Multi-year, fixed |

The non-obvious move: **selective volume-based contracts in commodity-specific patterns.** Not all commodities benefit from multi-year contracts equally. Chemicals + intermodal + autos are good candidates; coal + grain require more flexibility due to volume volatility.

## Recommendation skeleton

**Recommendation:** **Differentiated contract strategy by commodity.** Multi-year volume-based contracts for chemicals (3-5 years), intermodal (3 years), and finished goods (5 years) — covering ~50% of revenue. Maintain annual or spot pricing for grain + coal where volume volatility is high. Use volume commitments to optimize asset deployment in stable categories. Target operating ratio improvement of 3-4 percentage points within 3 years.

**Three supports:**

1. Volume-stable commodities benefit most from multi-year contract structure.
2. Volatile commodities require pricing flexibility to manage shipper relationships.
3. Asset productivity gains in stable categories fund competitive advantages in volatile ones.

**One risk:** multi-year fixed-price contracts expose railroad to inflation risk. Mitigate with fuel surcharges + indexation clauses on multi-year contracts.

## What judges look for

- Did you analyze **commodity-by-commodity** rather than treating freight uniformly?
- Did you identify **asset productivity** as the key benefit of volume predictability?
- Did you propose **indexation/escalators** to manage inflation risk?
- Did you size the **operating ratio impact** as the strategic outcome?
