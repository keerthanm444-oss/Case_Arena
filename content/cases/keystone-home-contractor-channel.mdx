## The situation

The "should we serve contractors" question is one of the most-studied B2B-extension cases for consumer-facing retailers. The opportunity is real ($3B addressable) and the existing infrastructure (stores, inventory, brand) provides some leverage. But contractor business is operationally distinct from DIY in ways that aren't obvious to anyone who hasn't worked the channel.

The contractor channel is **service-led, not product-led.** Contractors choose suppliers based on: stock availability for what they need today, credit terms, will-call/delivery reliability, and relationships with the inside-sales rep. Price matters but isn't decisive.

Three branches:

1. **Is the market attractive?** — $3B in-footprint TAM, growing modestly with construction activity, currently captured 80% by 2 player groups (independent supply + a single national competitor).
2. **Can we win?** — Keystone has store network (advantage: density), inventory breadth (advantage), brand (neutral — contractors care less than consumers); lacks B2B operations (credit, delivery, account management), inside-sales force, specialty SKUs contractors need.
3. **How to enter?** — three options: (a) integrate into existing stores with a "Pro desk", (b) build dedicated contractor-only stores in selected markets, (c) acquire a regional contractor supply chain.

## Key analysis

The diagnostic question: **what's the operational gap between current capability and contractor-winning capability?**

Most retailers in this position discover three large gaps:

1. **Inventory** — contractor SKU mix is different from DIY (commercial-grade rather than consumer-grade; larger pack sizes; more specialty items). Current store inventory covers maybe 60-70% of contractor needs.
2. **Service** — contractors need will-call/delivery within hours, not days. Building this is a logistics rebuild, not a process tweak.
3. **Credit + AR** — extending 30-90 day net terms to thousands of small contractors is a credit-underwriting operation Keystone doesn't have.

The economic gap typically looks like: contractor gross margin is 4-8 points lower than DIY (price sensitivity) but average order size is 5-10x larger (higher absolute contribution per visit). The two-sided question is whether the operational investment pays back.

A typical pattern:<Citation publisher="Case in Point" title="Case in Point — Complete Case Interview Preparation" authors={['Cosentino, Marc']} year={2023} /> Pro-desk integration captures roughly 15-25% of in-footprint contractor share over 3-5 years; dedicated contractor stores can capture 30-40% but require 2-3 years to break even per store; acquisition can be faster but rarely available at reasonable valuations.

## Recommendation skeleton

**Recommendation:** Pursue a **two-phase strategy**: Pro-desk integration in the top 100 stores immediately (~$80M, 18-month rollout), with parallel build of 10 dedicated contractor stores in highest-density contractor markets (~$120M, 30-month rollout). Re-evaluate at month 24 before scaling either further.

**Three supports:**

1. Pro-desk integration is fast + cheap and captures the easiest contractor share without requiring full operational rebuild.
2. Dedicated contractor stores in selected markets test the higher-investment model in lower-risk geography first.
3. Two-phase reduces all-in commitment — if Pro-desk underperforms, dedicated store rollout pauses.

**One risk:** the inside-sales-rep talent pool for contractor channels is limited; growing too fast means under-staffing the relationships that contractors value. Mitigate with explicit hiring milestones gating store rollout speed.

## What judges look for

- Did you identify **the three operational gaps** (inventory, service, credit) explicitly?
- Did you reject the "just add contractor pricing" frame? This is operations work, not pricing work.
- Did you propose **two-phase**, not all-in? Acquiring contractor share at scale requires capabilities Keystone doesn't yet have.
- Did you call out **inside-sales talent** as the binding constraint? Most candidates miss this.
