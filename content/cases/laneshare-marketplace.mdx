## The situation

The chicken-and-egg problem is the canonical marketplace launch challenge. The standard solutions are well-documented: pick a side to seed, concentrate geographically, subsidize the harder side, build "fake" supply if necessary.

The Laneshare-specific question is **which side to seed first** and **how geographically concentrated** to launch.

## Structuring

Marketplace-specific framework:

<IssueTreeEmbed slug="laneshare-tree" height={400} />

Five sub-questions:

1. **Which side is harder to acquire?** Usually the side with better alternatives (the side with less to gain from your platform). For freight, shippers usually have established broker relationships; small carriers usually take whatever load is available. Carriers are easier to seed; shippers are harder.

2. **What's the minimum viable concentration?** A marketplace works when supply density on the seeded side matches the demand pattern. For freight, this means concentrating in specific *lanes* (e.g., Chicago-to-Atlanta) rather than nationally.

3. **What's the take-rate ceiling?** Pricing in marketplaces is constrained by what the harder-to-acquire side will tolerate. If shippers' default broker takes 15%, Laneshare's take rate sits below that.

4. **What network effects compound?** Same-side (more carriers attract more carriers) is weak in freight. Cross-side (more carriers attract more shippers, vice-versa) is strong. The launch needs to maximize cross-side, not same-side.

5. **How to handle "thin markets"?** Lanes where supply or demand is sparse → matching fails → users churn. Need to either subsidize matching, restrict scope, or accept the dropout.

## Key analysis

The non-obvious move: **launch in one lane (e.g., Chicago-Atlanta), not nationally.**

Two reasons:

1. **Density beats breadth.** A marketplace with 100 carriers + 50 shippers in one lane works; 10,000 carriers + 5,000 shippers spread across the US doesn't, because the probability of finding a match for any single load is too low.

2. **Validates unit economics before scale.** If take rate × match rate × load value doesn't produce viable unit economics in one lane, scaling won't fix it. National launch burns cash on units that don't work.

Pick the lane based on three criteria:
- Lane volume large enough to matter (top 10 US lanes by volume)
- Lane fragmentation (lots of small carriers, not consolidated)
- Lane geography (point-to-point lanes are simpler than networks)

Chicago-Atlanta, LA-Phoenix, Houston-Dallas are typical fits.

## Recommendation skeleton

**Recommendation:** Launch in a single high-volume lane (recommend Chicago-Atlanta) for 6 months. Subsidize carrier-side onboarding aggressively (~$20M of the $30M budget). Target 200 active carriers + 50 active shippers + 80% match rate before expanding to the second lane. Use month 6-12 to add 2 more lanes; reserve final 6 months for measurement + national-expansion decision.

**Three supports:**

1. Single-lane density is required for the marketplace mechanics to work; national launch dilutes the network effect.
2. Carrier-side is the easier side; subsidizing carriers builds the supply shippers need to find the marketplace useful.
3. 18 months of runway is enough for 2-3 lanes + a national-decision data point; trying for national in 18 months over-extends.

**One risk:** A competitor (existing broker or another platform) launches in the same lane and out-subsidizes us. Mitigate by moving fast on carrier-side onboarding (week 1-8) and building switching costs (carrier-side data, customer relationships) before competitive response.

## What judges look for

- Did you identify **which side is harder to acquire** (shippers) and **which side to seed** (carriers)?
- Did you propose **geographic concentration** (single lane) rather than national launch?
- Did you specify **measurable density targets** (200 carriers, 50 shippers, 80% match rate) rather than vague "validate" language?
- Did you allocate the $30M budget concretely rather than waving at it?
