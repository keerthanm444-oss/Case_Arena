## The situation

Prescription digital therapeutics (PDTs) sit between drugs and therapy in the healthcare reimbursement system, which has historically been a problem. Drug-channel pricing assumes PBM reimbursement that PDTs often don't get; therapy-channel pricing assumes a delivery model (clinician time) that PDTs don't have.

Several first-wave PDT companies have struggled with this exact pricing problem. The case rewards candidates who recognize that **pricing strategy is downstream of channel strategy**.

## Structuring

Three branches:

1. **Customer (who pays)** — patient OOP, employer, PBM, health plan. Each channel has different willingness to pay and different sales motion.
2. **Competition (what's the alternative)** — generic sleep aids (~$10/month, low efficacy), CBT-I therapy ($150-200/session × 6-8 sessions, hard to access), or untreated (the most common alternative).
3. **Company (cost structure)** — variable cost per patient ~$10-30; fixed costs in clinical evidence + FDA + sales force. Margin structure is closer to software than pharma.

## Key analysis

The pivot question: **who's actually going to pay, and what do they value?**

For each potential payer, sketched economics:

- **Patient OOP** — sustainable only at $20-50/month for chronic conditions, much less for episodic. Acquisition cost dominates economics.
- **Employer (self-insured)** — willing to pay $500-2,000 per qualifying employee for a clinically proven intervention with productivity benefits. Sleep is a known productivity driver.
- **PBM / pharmacy benefit** — pricing per Rx; reimbursement currently inconsistent for PDTs. Long sales cycle.
- **Medical benefit (insurance)** — pricing per episode; requires payer contracting. Longer sales cycle.

The non-obvious finding: **employer-direct** is often the highest-margin channel for PDTs. Employers care about productivity + ROI, can decide quickly, and pay annually for a "covered employees" deal. Pharma-channel approaches that try to compete with drugs typically lose on the reimbursement game.

## Recommendation skeleton

**Recommendation:** Lead with **employer-direct go-to-market** at $1,200 per qualifying employee per year (covers unlimited course-based usage). Maintain a separate $300 per-course direct-to-patient channel for cash-pay patients. Pursue PBM reimbursement as a longer-term track (24+ months to first contract).

**Three supports:**

1. Employers are the lowest-friction payer for digital therapeutics — they can decide quickly + pay per-employee annually.
2. $1,200 per employee positions Luminath as an HR-benefit cost rather than a pharmacy cost, where the alternatives are absenteeism + presenteeism.
3. Separating cash-pay + employer channels avoids reference-price problems while maintaining patient access.

**One risk:** the employer-direct sales cycle is 6-12 months and requires building a sales force the company doesn't have. Mitigate by partnering with an existing benefits broker network in year 1, then building direct sales in year 2-3.

## What judges look for

- Did you recognize that **pricing strategy is downstream of channel strategy**?
- Did you identify **employer-direct as the highest-margin path** for PDTs?
- Did you reject the **pharma-channel default**? Most candidates anchor on drug-channel reimbursement.
- Did you propose a **multi-channel strategy** rather than committing to a single payer?
