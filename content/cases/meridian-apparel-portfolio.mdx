## The situation

Meridian's portfolio looks like many multi-brand apparel companies that grew via acquisition: a mix of strong brands subsidizing weak ones, shared infrastructure that's hard to allocate cleanly, and a CEO who's been protecting under-performers because divestment is operationally messy.

The board's question is correct — the portfolio should be rationalized — but the answer requires more than "drop the worst-margin brands."

## Structuring

Two-by-two on **growth rate × operating margin**, with explicit handling for **shared-cost dependencies**:

<IssueTreeEmbed slug="meridian-tree" height={400} />

For each of the 12 brands, classify:

| Growth | Margin | Action |
|--------|--------|--------|
| High | High | **Invest** — these are the keepers |
| High | Low | **Fix** — investigate why margin is low; may be growth-investment dilution |
| Low | High | **Harvest** — extract cash, no growth investment, eventual sale or wind-down |
| Low | Low | **Divest** — but check shared-cost dependencies before unwinding |

The two non-obvious moves:

1. **Mass-market or premium concentration?** Multi-brand portfolios that span 12 price tiers usually under-perform single-tier focused portfolios. Pick a tier and own it.
2. **Sourcing concentration.** If 4 brands share a manufacturer and you divest 2, the remaining 2 lose volume leverage. The order of divestment matters.

## Key analysis

A typical pattern in this portfolio dynamic:<Citation publisher="Case in Point" title="Case in Point — Complete Case Interview Preparation" authors={['Cosentino, Marc']} year={2023} />

- 3-4 brands generate ~70% of operating profit
- 5-6 brands generate the remaining ~30%
- 2-3 brands are persistently unprofitable

The standard mistake is to drop all 2-3 unprofitable brands. The correct move is more nuanced:

- **Drop only the ones that are unprofitable *and* have no shared-cost dependency.** Maybe 1-2 of the 3.
- **Hold the others until shared infrastructure can be redistributed.** Selling a brand that uses 30% of warehouse capacity stranded those costs.
- **Sequence divestments to preserve volume leverage** with shared suppliers.

## Recommendation skeleton

**Recommendation:** Reduce from 12 to 7 brands over 36 months. Divest 3 brands in year 1, wind down 2 in year 2. Concentrate the remaining 7 in the mid-premium tier ($60-300). Target operating margin: 9% by year 3.

**Three supports:**

1. The 7 retained brands cover the strategic tier with minimal customer-base overlap (no brand cannibalizes another).
2. Sequencing the divestments preserves supplier volume leverage and avoids stranded back-office costs.
3. The mid-premium concentration aligns with Meridian's capital-allocation reality — luxury requires marketing budgets we don't have; budget tier requires unit-economics we don't have.

**One risk:** the divestiture market for apparel brands is illiquid; we may need to take below-target sale prices. Mitigate by having operational improvement plans ready for each divestment candidate before they go to market.

## What judges look for

- Did you go beyond "drop the unprofitable ones" to address the **shared-cost** complication?
- Did you propose a **sequence** for divestment, not a simultaneous unwind?
- Did you specify the **target portfolio** (7 brands, $60-300 tier) rather than leaving the answer at "fewer brands"?
- Did you call out divestment market illiquidity as a risk + mitigation?
