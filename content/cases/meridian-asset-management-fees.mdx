## The situation

Active equity asset management has been in structural decline for 15+ years. Passive funds have captured most of the growth in retail and DC retirement assets; fee competition has compressed even the best active managers' fees. The strategic options for traditional active managers are well-rehearsed and well-tested.

The honest framing: there is no path to defending active equity as 70% of business. Diversification away from active equity is required.

Three branches:

1. **Existing business reality** — active equity is in secular decline; fee compression is structural. Question is rate of decline, not whether decline.
2. **Strategic options** — passive/ETF entry, alternatives expansion, M&A consolidation. Each has different capital requirements and time horizons.
3. **Capability fit** — what does Meridian already do well that translates to these options? Active investment skill ≠ ETF operations ≠ alternatives investing.

## Key analysis

Walking the three options:

**Acquire passive/ETF firm:**
- Capital: $2-5B for a meaningful platform
- Time to scale: immediate (acquired AUM contributes day 1)
- Strategic value: balance the mix, demonstrate distribution-broker viability
- Risk: passive has its own fee compression; acquiring late means buying at high multiples
- Cultural challenge: ETF operations vs active investing are very different businesses

**Shift to alternatives + private credit:**
- Capital: $200-500M to build / hire alternatives team
- Time to meaningful scale: 5-10 years
- Strategic value: significantly higher fees (1-2% management + 15-20% carry on private credit / PE)
- Risk: building alternatives capability organically is slow; track record requires 5-7 year fund cycles
- Cultural fit: closer to existing active-equity capabilities

**M&A with another active manager:**
- Captures scale economies (combined cost base, distribution overlap)
- Doesn't solve the underlying product-mix problem
- Multiple compression risk: combining two declining businesses

The non-obvious move: **alternatives expansion via acquisition rather than organic build.** Acquiring an established alternatives boutique ($10-30B AUM, 50-150 people) shortcuts the 5-10 year organic build. Multiples for alternatives boutiques are higher but justified by recurring management fees + carry.

## Recommendation skeleton

**Recommendation:** **Two-track strategy.** Track 1: acquire 1-2 alternatives boutiques ($30-50B combined AUM acquisition; $3-5B deployed capital). Track 2: optimize the active equity business — consolidate underperforming strategies, defend top-quartile strategies with marketing. Skip the passive/ETF acquisition. 5-year target: alternatives at 30-35% of AUM, 50%+ of fee revenue.

**Three supports:**

1. Alternatives acquisition captures higher-fee revenue that passive/ETF acquisition cannot.
2. Alternatives capability is closer to existing active-investment culture than passive operations would be.
3. Skipping passive acquisition avoids overpaying for a category that's already fee-compressed.

**One risk:** alternatives acquisition multiples are at historical highs; we may pay too much. Mitigate by patient deployment — willing to walk from auctions, focus on bilateral negotiations with founders looking for succession + capital partnerships.

## What judges look for

- Did you accept **active equity decline as structural** rather than trying to defend it?
- Did you size **alternatives capability** as the best strategic option for Meridian specifically?
- Did you reject **passive/ETF acquisition** despite its surface appeal?
- Did you address **acquisition multiple risk** with patient-deployment discipline?
