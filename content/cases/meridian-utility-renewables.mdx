## The situation

Regulated electric utility transition is one of the largest capital-allocation decisions in any industry. Utilities operate under cost-of-service regulation where capex earns a regulated return — meaning they're financially incentivized to spend, but rate impact must be approved by regulators. The transition decision interacts with three regulatory constraints: prudence (was capex reasonable?), rate impact (are customer bills tolerable?), and reliability (does generation match demand?).

Three branches:

1. **Regulatory environment** — what does each state commission approve? Some states (CA, NY, MA) push aggressive transition; others (TX, WV) emphasize cost protection.
2. **Generation portfolio economics** — coal retirement costs (stranded asset write-downs vs depreciation runway), gas as bridge, renewable + storage costs, nuclear life extension.
3. **Transmission + customer impact** — grid investments to integrate renewables, customer rate trajectories, large-customer commitments (data centers, manufacturing).

## Key analysis

Comparing the three options:

| Plan | Capex | Rate impact | Coal retirement | Risk profile |
|------|-------|-------------|------------------|--------------|
| Aggressive | $25B+ | +35-50% over 15 yrs | By 2030 | Stranded asset write-downs, customer pushback |
| Moderate | $15B | +20-30% over 15 yrs | By 2035 | Slower regulatory recovery, gas-asset risk |
| Minimal | $8B | +10-15% over 15 yrs | Past 2040 | Federal/state mandate exposure, customer demand mismatch |

The non-obvious finding: **moderate transition with optionality is usually the right answer.** Aggressive transition is over-confident about regulatory approval of rate increases; minimal transition is over-confident that regulatory + customer pressure won't accelerate.

For Meridian: moderate plan with built-in acceleration triggers — invest in renewable + storage flexibly, retire coal on a schedule aligned with depreciation + alternative-generation availability, position to accelerate if regulatory environment hardens or large customers commit to renewable supply.

## Recommendation skeleton

**Recommendation:** **Moderate transition with optionality.** 15-year plan: $15B base case, with $5B "accelerator" budget triggered if (a) federal mandates tighten, (b) major customers commit to renewable supply, (c) regulators in specific states grant accelerated cost recovery. Coal retirement schedule tied to depreciation completion (2030-2035) plus renewable + storage availability. Gas as bridge generation for capacity firming, not long-term build-out.

**Three supports:**

1. Moderate base case avoids stranded-asset write-downs and customer-rate shock.
2. Optionality preserves the ability to accelerate if conditions change.
3. Phasing tied to depreciation completion minimizes regulatory disallowance risk on coal retirements.

**One risk:** if federal carbon pricing emerges aggressively (e.g., $100+/ton), moderate pace becomes uneconomic. Mitigate with the explicit accelerator-budget mechanism designed to deploy capital quickly if conditions shift.

## What judges look for

- Did you balance **regulatory approval + customer rate impact + ESG pressure** as joint constraints?
- Did you propose **optionality** rather than commit to a single path?
- Did you tie **coal retirement to depreciation schedules** to manage write-downs?
- Did you identify **gas as bridge** rather than long-term build?
