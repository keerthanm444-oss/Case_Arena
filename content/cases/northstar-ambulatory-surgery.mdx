## The situation

The hospital-to-ASC procedural shift is one of the most significant structural trends in US healthcare delivery over the past decade. CMS reimbursement decisions have moved hundreds of procedures to ASC-eligible status; cost differentials (40-60% lower at ASCs vs hospitals) drive payer + employer preference; outcomes data is generally favorable.

The strategic question isn't whether to grow — the tailwind is real — but **how to grow given the binding constraint of surgeon recruitment.**

Three branches:

1. **Where to grow** — geographic + specialty prioritization. Orthopedic has the largest ASC-shift opportunity; cardiology is high-margin but smaller volumes; GI is high-volume but low-margin.
2. **How to grow** — organic (build new centers; 18-24 month timeline), acquire independent ASCs (faster, capital-intensive), JV with health system (lower capital, brings surgeon relationships).
3. **What enables growth** — surgeon recruitment (the actual constraint), payer contracting, regulatory approval (Certificate of Need in some states).

## Key analysis

Two diagnostic numbers:

**Number 1 — surgeon supply per ASC.** A typical ASC requires 8-15 surgeons routinely operating there. Competing for surgeon time across an expanding network is harder than competing for patients. In markets where independent surgeon groups already have ASC affiliations, recruitment is structurally difficult.

**Number 2 — specialty-specific unit economics.**

| Specialty | Typical case volume | Revenue per case | 4-wall margin at scale |
|-----------|---------------------|------------------|------------------------|
| Orthopedic (joint replacement) | Lower | $25-40K | 30-40% |
| Cardiology | Medium | $15-25K | 20-30% |
| GI / Endoscopy | High | $1-3K | 15-25% |

The non-obvious move: **JVs with health systems are often the right answer** for specialties where the surgeon supply is in hospital-employed groups. Building a competing ASC in a market where the hospital owns the surgeons is a recruitment failure pattern.

Acquisition is fast (4-8 months per deal) but multiples have compressed; independent ASCs in good markets often sell at 10-12x EBITDA vs build-cost equivalents of 6-8x EBITDA over time.

## Recommendation skeleton

**Recommendation:** Three-track growth over 5 years. Track A: 8-10 organic builds in high-priority orthopedic markets where surgeon supply is independent (~$200M capex). Track B: 6-10 acquisitions of independent ASCs in markets where build timelines are slow (~$300M). Track C: 4-6 JVs with health systems in markets where hospital-employed surgeon supply dominates (~$80M).

**Three supports:**

1. Multi-track strategy matches the entry mode to the specific market dynamics; one-size-fits-all underperforms.
2. Orthopedic-first organic builds capture the highest-margin growth where surgeon recruitment is feasible.
3. Health-system JVs unlock markets that pure-play ASCs can't enter due to surgeon-supply concentration.

**One risk:** the surgeon-recruitment market is the binding constraint and may not scale linearly with capital. Mitigate by recruiting surgeon leaders 12 months ahead of capital deployment in each market.

## What judges look for

- Did you identify **surgeon supply as the binding constraint**, not capital or patient demand?
- Did you propose a **multi-track strategy** (organic + acquisition + JV) matched to market dynamics?
- Did you size **specialty-specific unit economics** rather than treat "ASC growth" as uniform?
- Did you recognize that **health-system JVs** are sometimes the only way into specific markets?
