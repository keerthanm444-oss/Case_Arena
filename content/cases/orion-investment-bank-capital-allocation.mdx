## The situation

Equities trading at sub-cost-of-capital ROE is a chronic problem for mid-sized investment banks. The top 3-5 global players have scale-driven cost advantages that mid-tier players cannot match. Several mid-sized banks have already exited equities trading entirely over the past decade with mixed strategic outcomes.

The decision depends critically on **how much equities serves as customer-service infrastructure for higher-margin businesses** versus a standalone profit center.

Three branches:

1. **Standalone ROE** — equities ROE at 6% vs cost of equity 11% = 500bps negative value creation. Roughly $300-500M annual economic loss for a business at this scale.
2. **Cross-business value** — how much M&A advisory revenue is conditional on equities support? How much capital-markets execution depends on trading capability?
3. **Strategic options** — full exit, scale-up to compete (requires significant capital deployment), maintain as customer service business with capped capital.

## Key analysis

The diagnostic question: **what share of M&A advisory revenue is at risk if equities exits?**

Typical patterns:

- **Pure M&A clients** (50-60% of advisory revenue) — don't require equities support; M&A relationships are decoupled.
- **Capital-markets clients** (30-40% of advisory revenue) — IPOs, follow-ons, block trades; these need equities execution capability.
- **Trading-symbiotic clients** (10-15%) — hedge funds, asset managers who use equities + advisory together.

If 40-50% of M&A advisory revenue is at risk from equities exit, the exit decision is much harder than the standalone ROE math suggests.

The non-obvious move: **scale-down rather than exit.** Keep equities as a minimal-capital customer-service business serving the capital-markets clients; cut the prop-trading + market-making book that consumes the bulk of capital but generates the smallest revenue contribution.

This preserves the cross-business value while drastically reducing the capital base, lifting effective ROE through reduced denominator rather than increased numerator.

## Recommendation skeleton

**Recommendation:** **Scale-down equities to client-service only.** Reduce trading book + market-making capital by 60-70% over 18 months. Maintain execution capability for capital-markets clients; exit pure prop-trading entirely. Reallocated capital ($3-5B) goes to: (1) M&A advisory expansion, (2) fixed-income credit business growth, (3) capital return to shareholders. Target firm-wide ROE: 12% by year 3.

**Three supports:**

1. The cross-business value of equities makes full exit risky; scale-down preserves it at much lower capital cost.
2. Capital reallocation to higher-ROE businesses (advisory, credit) directly lifts firm ROE.
3. The reduced equities footprint signals discipline to investors and removes the largest single drag on ROE.

**One risk:** clients may interpret scale-down as exit signal, leading to advisory mandate losses. Mitigate with proactive client communication, retention of senior research analysts (the visible part of equities for clients), and explicit "client service" positioning.

## What judges look for

- Did you reject **full equities exit** in favor of scale-down based on cross-business value?
- Did you size **M&A advisory revenue at risk** as the binding constraint on exit decisions?
- Did you propose **specific capital reallocation** rather than vague "use freed capital well"?
- Did you address the **client signaling risk** of scale-down?
