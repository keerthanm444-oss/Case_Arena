## The situation

Pinewood Grocer has been investing heavily in private-label expansion for three years. The strategic premise: private-label SKUs carry 8-12 percentage points higher gross margin than equivalent national brands, so growing private-label penetration should mechanically lift operating margin.

The data doesn't agree. Private-label is up (22% → 35% of basket value), revenue is up (6% YoY), but operating margin has compressed by 140 basis points. The CEO is asking what's wrong.

The three real branches to investigate:

1. **Has private-label GM held up?** Likely no. Introductory price points to drive trial often run at <25% GM in year 1; legacy assumption was that these would mature toward 36% over time.
2. **Has national brand GM dropped?** Suppliers often reduce trade promotion spend when the retailer's private-label competes directly. Lost trade dollars = lower effective GM on national brands.
3. **Has the operating cost base scaled with SKU expansion?** Private-label SKU count likely doubled — more warehouse complexity, more labor, more shrink. Operating margin captures this; gross margin does not.

## What the analysis usually shows

A typical pattern in this dynamic:

- Private-label GM has settled at **24-28%, not the originally projected 34-36%** — promotional pricing got sticky.
- National brand GM dropped 2-3 points as trade dollars contracted.
- Operating costs per unit rose ~5% from SKU complexity.

The combination compresses margin even with revenue growth and "favorable" mix shift.

## Recommendation skeleton

**Recommendation:** Halt private-label SKU expansion for 12 months. Rationalize the lowest-velocity 20% of private-label SKUs. Renegotiate trade terms with top 5 national brand suppliers under threat of further private-label substitution.

**Three supports:**

1. Current private-label GM (~25%) is too close to national brand GM (~28%) to justify the operational complexity premium.
2. SKU rationalization frees warehouse + labor capacity, addressing the operating-cost driver.
3. Trade-term renegotiation captures real dollars; existing private-label penetration gives Pinewood credible leverage.

**One risk:** suppliers retaliate by reducing slotting allowances or co-op marketing. Mitigate by sequencing the renegotiation — start with the supplier most exposed to Pinewood revenue.

## What judges look for

- Did you challenge the CEO's assumption that private-label growth automatically equals margin growth?
- Did you separate the **mix-shift effect** (mechanical) from the **margin-by-SKU effect** (operational)?
- Did you size the trade-promotion loss as a distinct line item rather than burying it in gross margin?
- Did you recommend a **pause + rationalize** before a full reversal? Reversing private-label strategy is much more expensive than pausing it.
