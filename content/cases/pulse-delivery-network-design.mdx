## The situation

Last-mile delivery is the highest-cost segment of e-commerce logistics — typically 40-60% of total fulfillment cost. The economics vary dramatically by population density: dense urban delivery can be 50-70% cheaper per package than suburban or rural. Specialist last-mile companies have historically thrived in dense cores; they struggle when expanding to lower-density geographies.

The strategic question is whether expansion is the right growth path or whether deepening urban dominance produces better economics.

Three branches:

1. **Geographic options** — urban core deepening (incremental capacity), suburban expansion (new geography), tier-2 city entry (parallel to existing cores).
2. **Service options** — same-day premium, scheduled delivery, refrigerated/specialty, large items, returns.
3. **Asset model** — own fleet, gig-economy contractors, hybrid.

## Key analysis

Density economics:

| Density | Stops per route per hour | Cost per delivery |
|---------|---------------------------|-------------------|
| Dense urban core | 8-12 | $3-5 |
| Standard urban | 5-7 | $7-10 |
| Suburban | 2-3 | $12-18 |
| Rural | 1-2 | $20-30+ |

The non-obvious finding: **suburban expansion typically destroys margins.** A 3x cost-per-delivery for suburban vs urban makes price-competitive operations difficult against incumbents (UPS, FedEx) who already have suburban networks built out.

For Pulse specifically: deepening urban dominance + adding specialty services (refrigerated, large items where competitors have weak offerings) preserves the structural cost advantage. Suburban expansion is a value-destroyer unless tied to clear customer commitments.

## Recommendation skeleton

**Recommendation:** **Deepen urban + add specialty services.** Phase 1: invest in micro-fulfillment + bike couriers in existing 15 metros, lifting stops-per-route 15-20% (margin improvement of 2-3 percentage points). Phase 2: launch refrigerated + large-item premium services where competitors are weak. Phase 3: selective tier-2 city entry only when anchor customer commits to volume. Defer suburban expansion.

**Three supports:**

1. Urban density is structural cost advantage; deepening compounds the moat.
2. Specialty services capture higher-margin premium without sacrificing density economics.
3. Suburban expansion would erode the cost advantage that makes Pulse competitive.

**One risk:** competitors may saturate urban cores faster than Pulse can deepen position. Mitigate by exclusive merchant partnerships in dense cores + customer-experience differentiation (1-hour windows, returns convenience).

## What judges look for

- Did you reject **suburban expansion** despite intuitive appeal?
- Did you size **density-driven cost differences** as the strategic constraint?
- Did you propose **specialty services** to expand revenue per delivery?
- Did you address **competitive saturation risk** in urban cores?
