## The situation

Convenience stores have an underappreciated profit structure: **fuel is a traffic driver, not a profit center.** The actual money is in the in-store basket — coffee, snacks, prepared food, tobacco, lottery. EV adoption threatens not the fuel margin (which is small) but the **traffic** that drives in-store sales.

The strategic question becomes: how to maintain traffic as fuel-driven visits decline.

Three sub-questions:

1. **Fuel traffic decay curve** — how fast does EV adoption translate to fuel-station visit decline in our specific markets? Urban + coastal markets decline first; rural Midwest decades later.
2. **In-store traffic substitution** — can we attract customers without the fuel anchor? Destination items (specialty coffee, food, alcohol) drive traffic on their own.
3. **Charging infrastructure economics** — does owning fast-chargers replace fuel-station traffic? Probably not on the same economics; charging time is 15-45 minutes vs 5 minutes for fuel, so utilization per stall is much lower.

## Key analysis

Two diagnostic numbers:

**Number 1 — share of in-store revenue from fuel customers:** If 70%+ of in-store basket comes from fuel customers, in-store revenue will decay nearly as fast as fuel. If 30-40%, the in-store business has independent traffic and can sustain.

**Number 2 — geographic fuel-decay distribution:** Urban stores might see 40% fuel decline by 2030; rural Midwest stores might see <10%. National strategy averaged across the network misleads.

A typical pattern for a Midwest convenience chain: ~55-65% of in-store revenue is fuel-driven, urban stores represent ~20% of the network but ~30% of revenue, and rural stores have 10+ years before meaningful EV penetration.

The combination suggests:

- **Urban stores need pivoting** — destination food, dwell-friendly amenities, possibly fast-charging where utilization can support it.
- **Rural stores stay the course** — fuel-anchored convenience for 10+ more years.
- **Sell now option** depends entirely on what buyers value the chain at — and most strategic buyers are also facing fuel decline.

## Recommendation skeleton

**Recommendation:** Reject the "sell now" option (timing already past peak). Pursue a **two-track strategy**: pivot the urban 80 stores toward destination retail + selective charging, hold rural 320 stores as a traditional convenience-fuel operation for 5-10 years. Re-evaluate in 36 months.

**Three supports:**

1. Geographic fuel-decay curves are too different to apply a single strategy nationally — the urban/rural split exists for good economic reasons.
2. The rural 320 stores remain a cash-generating asset for 5-10 more years; selling them now under-monetizes the runway.
3. Pivoting urban stores to destination retail addresses the actual risk (traffic loss) rather than the surface symptom (fuel-margin compression).

**One risk:** the destination-retail pivot requires real-estate refresh + product-mix changes that historically have low success rates in convenience-store conversions. Mitigate by piloting in 10 urban stores before scaling.

## What judges look for

- Did you recognize that **fuel is the traffic driver, not the profit center**? This is the central reframe.
- Did you propose **geographic differentiation** rather than a single national strategy?
- Did you reject the "sell at peak" framing? Most candidates accept the CEO's frame uncritically.
- Did you size **the urban-vs-rural decay curve** rather than treating EV adoption as uniform?
