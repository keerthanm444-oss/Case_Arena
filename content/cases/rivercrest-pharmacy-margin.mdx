## The situation

Pharmacy retail is one of the most studied price-taker industries. PBMs (pharmacy benefit managers) negotiate reimbursement rates on behalf of insurers; for most regional chains, the choice is "accept the rate or lose the contract." Reimbursement compression has been a structural trend; the question is whether Rivercrest can adapt to it or needs to exit.

The key reframe: **prescriptions are increasingly a traffic driver, not a profit center.** The profitable business is what customers buy while they're in the store for the prescription.

Three branches:

1. **Prescription economics** — mostly fixed by PBM contracts; small levers in generic substitution rates and dispensing efficiency.
2. **Front-of-store revenue** — non-prescription purchases (OTC, beauty, convenience). This is where Rivercrest controls margin.
3. **New revenue streams** — vaccinations, basic clinical services (where regulation permits), MTM (medication therapy management) consults.

## Key analysis

The diagnostic test: **revenue per pharmacy visit broken into prescription + non-prescription buckets.** If non-prescription revenue per visit has dropped or stayed flat while prescription volume grew, the chain is failing to convert pharmacy traffic into front-of-store sales.

Industry benchmarks:

- Prescription gross margin: $3-8 per Rx for branded, $5-10 for generic (Rivercrest probably has limited variability here)
- Non-prescription revenue per pharmacy visit: $4-15 (high variability; depends on store layout, basket-building tactics)
- Clinical services revenue per visit: $0-50 (depends entirely on whether the chain offers vaccinations + MTM)

A typical Rivercrest-like pattern: prescription gross margin has fallen ~$2 per Rx, front-of-store revenue per visit is flat (when it should be growing with traffic), clinical services are offered in <20% of stores.

The compounding insight: if Rivercrest's strategy treats prescriptions as the product and front-of-store as the loss leader, the strategy is backwards. The economics now favor treating prescriptions as customer acquisition and front-of-store as the monetization layer.

## Recommendation skeleton

**Recommendation:** Reposition the front-of-store as the profit center over 24 months. Three interventions: (1) re-merchandise pharmacy waiting areas to drive incremental basket spend, (2) expand vaccinations + MTM to 80% of stores (currently <20%), (3) consolidate the bottom-decile stores where prescription volume can't justify the fixed cost.

**Three supports:**

1. Prescription margin compression is structural — fixing it requires industry-level leverage Rivercrest doesn't have.
2. Front-of-store has 4-7x the margin variability of prescriptions; small wins here compound quickly.
3. Clinical services are a high-margin growth lever that's underutilized at 20% store penetration.

**One risk:** clinical services require pharmacist training + state-by-state regulatory compliance. Mitigate with a phased rollout starting in states with the most permissive scope-of-practice rules.

## What judges look for

- Did you recognize that **PBM reimbursement is largely exogenous** to Rivercrest — it's a price-taker industry?
- Did you reframe the strategic question from "fix prescription margin" to "monetize pharmacy traffic differently"?
- Did you size **front-of-store revenue per visit** as the key lever, not prescription gross margin?
- Did you identify **store consolidation** as part of the answer — fewer, better-monetized stores often beats more, lower-economics stores?
