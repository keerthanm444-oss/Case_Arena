## The situation

Nursing-home economics are tight and getting tighter: Medicare reimbursement caps, Medicaid pays below cost in many states, private-pay willingness varies widely by region and STAR rating. A 7-point occupancy gap (78% vs 85% peers) translates to roughly 30-40% of operating profit on an industry P&L because variable costs (food, utilities) scale with occupancy while fixed costs (nursing labor, building) don't.

The 35 underperformers are doing most of the damage; the question is what to do about them specifically.

## Structuring

For each of the 35 underperformers, classify:

1. **Market viability** — Is the local elderly population declining, stable, or growing? Are there other SNF operators in the trade area?
2. **Operational viability** — Is the home structurally inferior (old, undersized) or just operationally poor (staffing, STAR rating, clinical quality)?
3. **Payer mix** — Heavy Medicaid (low margin even at full occupancy) or balanced (more upside)?

A typical breakdown:

- 10-12 homes: declining markets, structurally inferior → **close**
- 10-12 homes: stable markets, operationally fixable → **invest** (STAR rating, staffing)
- 8-10 homes: heavy Medicaid mix in average markets → **maintain** but cap investment
- 3-5 homes: strategic geographic positions, fixable → **invest heavily**

## Key analysis

The two diagnostic measurements:

**Diagnostic 1 — STAR rating distribution:** SNF residents and families now check STAR ratings before placement. A 2-star home in a competitive market has structurally lower demand regardless of operational quality. STAR improvement takes 12-24 months but is the most leveraged single fix.

**Diagnostic 2 — payer mix marginal analysis:** Filling a Medicaid bed at $200/day vs filling a private-pay bed at $400/day: at 90% occupancy ceiling, the private-pay bed contributes ~2x more. Some "underperforming" homes are underperforming on revenue per bed, not occupancy.

The non-obvious finding in most analyses: closing 10 homes recovers margin faster than improving 20 homes, even though the political + community optics are harder.

## Recommendation skeleton

**Recommendation:** Close 10 homes in declining markets (year 1). Concentrate STAR-rating investment in 15 homes with good market dynamics + fixable operational issues (target: 2-3 → 4 stars in 18 months). Maintain the remaining 10 underperformers at minimum investment; revisit at year 3. Goal: consolidated occupancy 85% + operating margin 4% by year 3.

**Three supports:**

1. The 10 to close cannot be saved by operational improvements — market dynamics are against them.
2. The 15 to invest in have demonstrable path to improvement (STAR rating is the single highest-leverage lever in SNF).
3. Maintaining the remaining 10 preserves optionality while targeting limited capital at the highest-return improvements.

**One risk:** community + regulatory opposition to closures can delay the wind-down by 12-18 months per home. Mitigate by initiating closures in states with the most lenient closure regulations first; sequence over 24 months to manage community optics.

## What judges look for

- Did you classify the **35 homes into multiple sub-segments** rather than treating them as a single problem?
- Did you identify **STAR ratings as the highest-leverage operational lever**?
- Did you accept that **closures are part of the answer** despite the community optics?
- Did you size the **payer-mix asymmetry** (private-pay vs Medicaid) explicitly?
