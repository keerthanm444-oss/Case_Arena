## The situation

Airline route economics are notoriously complex. Standalone route profitability misleads — many "unprofitable" routes feed profitable hub connections; many "profitable" routes depend on feeder volume from the unprofitable ones. The strategic question requires network-level analysis, not route-by-route.

Three branches:

1. **Route classification** — pure point-to-point unprofitable (clean exit candidates), hub-feeders unprofitable on standalone but contributing to hub profitability, frequency-sensitive routes (could improve with adjustment).
2. **Hub network impact** — exit of feeders weakens hub connectivity; non-trivial second-order revenue impact.
3. **Operational redeployment** — where do exited aircraft + crew + gates redeploy? Can fleet be sold?

## Key analysis

A typical 60-unprofitable-routes decomposition:

| Route type | Count | Action |
|------------|-------|--------|
| Pure point-to-point, structurally unprofitable | 15-25 | **Exit** |
| Hub feeders with positive network contribution | 20-30 | Keep — losses recovered by hub spillover |
| Marginal — small fleet/frequency improvements would fix | 10-15 | Adjust frequency or aircraft type |
| Strategic (competitive blocking, growth markets) | 5-10 | Maintain selectively |

The non-obvious finding: **route-level losses understate hub feeder value.** A feeder route losing $2M/year might contribute $4M in passengers that connect through the hub to long-haul flights at high margins. Exit kills both.

The diagnostic test for hub feeders: calculate network contribution per route (standalone profit + connecting passenger revenue × connection share). Many "unprofitable" routes turn out to be net contributors.

## Recommendation skeleton

**Recommendation:** **Selective exit of 20-25 routes over 18 months.** Exit only routes meeting two criteria: (1) standalone unprofitable AND (2) <20% of passengers connect to onward flights at hubs. Adjust frequency on additional 15-20 marginal routes. Redeploy aircraft + slots to growth opportunities (new routes in growing geographies or higher-frequency on profitable existing routes).

**Three supports:**

1. Network-contribution analysis filters out exit candidates that look unprofitable but contribute to hub economics.
2. Frequency adjustment captures cost savings without losing market presence.
3. Aircraft redeployment preserves fleet value rather than parking aircraft.

**One risk:** exited routes may be picked up by competitors, eroding market position long-term. Mitigate by maintaining slot rights + frequent flyer connectivity (e.g., through codeshare partners) where strategic.

## What judges look for

- Did you reject **route-level profitability** as the sole exit criterion?
- Did you propose **network-contribution analysis** for hub-feeder routes?
- Did you size **frequency adjustment** as a middle-ground action?
- Did you address **redeployment economics** of exited assets?
