## The situation

State Medicaid cost growth is one of the largest budget pressures facing US states. The drivers — healthcare cost inflation, eligibility expansion, aging demographics, chronic disease prevalence — are largely structural. State control levers are limited: rates (limited by access concerns), eligibility (limited by federal rules + political constraints), benefits (limited by federal mandates), program design (managed care, value-based contracting).

The realistic playbook combines multiple levers; no single lever produces 3+ percentage points of growth reduction.

Three branches:

1. **High-cost beneficiary management** — top 5% drive 50%+ of costs; care management + chronic disease programs target this segment.
2. **Program design** — managed care expansion, value-based contracting with MCOs, capitation models.
3. **Operations** — eligibility verification, program integrity (fraud + abuse), provider rate negotiation, prior authorization.

## Key analysis

Typical Medicaid cost decomposition:

| Beneficiary group | % beneficiaries | % cost |
|-------------------|-----------------|--------|
| Healthy adults + children | 70% | 25% |
| Chronic disease (1-3 conditions) | 20% | 35% |
| Complex chronic (4+ conditions) | 7% | 25% |
| End-of-life / catastrophic | 3% | 15% |

The non-obvious move: **target the 7% complex chronic segment with intensive care management.** This segment is large enough to matter (25% of cost), drives most of the cost growth, and responds well to care coordination (medication reconciliation, social determinants intervention, transitional care).

For the state specifically: $18B × 25% = $4.5B in complex-chronic spend. Even 10% reduction in this segment via intensive care management = $450M annual savings (2.5 percentage points of cost growth reduction). Combined with other levers, achieving the 3-percentage-point reduction target becomes realistic.

## Recommendation skeleton

**Recommendation:** **Multi-track Medicaid reform over 3 years.** Track 1: launch intensive care management for top 7% complex-chronic beneficiaries ($300-500M annual savings at maturity). Track 2: expand managed care from 50% to 75% of beneficiaries (~$200-400M savings, longer ramp). Track 3: shift MCO contracts to value-based payment for top conditions (diabetes, heart failure, COPD). Track 4: enhanced program integrity ($100-200M savings). Total: 3-3.5 percentage points cost growth reduction by year 3.

**Three supports:**

1. Care management targeting high-cost beneficiaries has highest savings ROI.
2. Managed care expansion captures predictable cost growth reduction over time.
3. Value-based contracting aligns MCO incentives with cost control.

**One risk:** care management programs may not achieve expected savings if implementation is rushed or population engagement is low. Mitigate by piloting in 2-3 regions before statewide rollout; explicit savings measurement protocol.

## What judges look for

- Did you identify **high-cost beneficiary concentration** as the priority lever?
- Did you propose **multi-track** rather than betting on single lever?
- Did you size **value-based contracting** as a structural improvement?
- Did you address **federal match implications** of cost reduction?
