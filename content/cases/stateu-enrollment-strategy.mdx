## The situation

Public university enrollment decline has been one of the most-studied demographic shifts of the past decade. The drivers — demographic cliff (US births declined 2007-2020 → 18-year-olds declining now), value perception (high tuition + low-perceived ROI), community-college substitution, online education growth — are mostly structural.

Different universities face very different futures depending on segment focus, geographic market, and cost structure flexibility.

## Structuring

Three branches:

1. **Enrollment segments** — traditional undergrad (declining), transfer + adult learners (more stable), international (growing but politically dependent), graduate (variable).
2. **Program portfolio** — which majors are growing (CS, business, nursing) vs declining (humanities, education)?
3. **Cost flexibility** — tenured faculty (fixed cost) vs adjunct (variable). Administrative overhead.

## Key analysis

Enrollment decomposition for a typical regional flagship in this position:

| Segment | Current | Trend | Action |
|---------|---------|-------|--------|
| Traditional in-state undergrad | 60% of enrollment | -3% YoY | Defend with selective investment |
| Out-of-state undergrad | 15% | -5% YoY | Maintain via aid + marketing |
| International undergrad | 8% | Volatile | Limited focus |
| Adult / transfer students | 10% | +2% YoY | **Major growth opportunity** |
| Graduate students | 7% | Flat | Selective program growth |

The non-obvious move: **adult + transfer students are the structurally growing segment** that flagship universities have historically under-served. Programs designed for working adults (evening, online, flexible scheduling) capture demand that traditional-residential programs miss.

For State University specifically: investing in adult/transfer-focused programs (online completion programs, certificate-to-degree pathways, accelerated formats) addresses the segment that's growing while traditional segments shrink.

Program rationalization: declining majors should be selectively consolidated; investment should flow to growth programs (CS, nursing, business). Tenured faculty constraints make this slow — typically 5-10 year horizon.

## Recommendation skeleton

**Recommendation:** **Three-track enrollment recovery over 5 years.** Track 1: invest $30M in adult-learner programs (online infrastructure, accelerated formats, employer partnerships) — target 30% adult/transfer share by year 5. Track 2: program portfolio rotation — expand growth majors (CS, nursing, business), wind down underperforming majors over 5-10 year cycles. Track 3: financial aid increase ($15M/year) to maintain traditional undergraduate yield. Target: enrollment recovery to 95% of peak by year 5.

**Three supports:**

1. Adult-learner focus addresses structurally growing segment vs defending declining traditional one.
2. Program portfolio rotation aligns offering with demand.
3. Aid increases offset competitive pressure from community colleges + private competitors.

**One risk:** tenured faculty in declining programs resist program changes. Mitigate by faculty retirement timing — many universities have age-distribution that creates natural attrition in 5-10 years; align program-restructuring with attrition.

## What judges look for

- Did you analyze **enrollment by segment** rather than treating decline as uniform?
- Did you identify **adult/transfer learners** as the growth opportunity?
- Did you propose **program rotation** rather than blanket cuts?
- Did you address **tenured faculty constraint** on cost flexibility?
