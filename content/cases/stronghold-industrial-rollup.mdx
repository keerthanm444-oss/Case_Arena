## The situation

PE-backed rollups of fragmented service industries (HVAC, plumbing, pest control, dental, vet care) have been one of the most active M&A categories of the past decade. The thesis is straightforward: many small businesses → consolidate → capture scale economies → multiple arbitrage on exit (small-business multiples 4-5x EBITDA → larger company multiples 8-12x).

The thesis often works on paper but fails in execution because **scale economies are operational, not financial.** Buying 8 companies and running them as 8 separate companies captures zero value.

Three branches:

1. **Integration scorecard** — for each acquired company, what's been centralized (billing, scheduling, dispatch, fleet, IT) vs left local?
2. **Customer retention** — what's the post-acquisition customer retention rate? Founder-operator HVAC businesses often have personal customer relationships that don't transfer well.
3. **Talent and leadership** — are the acquired company founders still active? When did they leave? What replaced them?

## Key analysis

The diagnostic that explains most underperforming rollups: **integration depth.**

Post-acquisition integration scorecard for a typical underperforming HVAC rollup:

| Function | Centralized? | Value at risk |
|----------|--------------|---------------|
| Billing / AR | Partial | Working capital tied up |
| Scheduling / dispatch | No | 15-25% lost productivity |
| Fleet management | No | 10-20% higher fleet cost |
| IT / software | No | Onboarding cost per acquisition stays high |
| Marketing / lead gen | No | CAC same as standalone |
| Purchasing (parts) | Partial | 5-10% missed savings |

If integration is 40-50% complete (typical for 18-month rollups), you've captured maybe 30% of the available value.

The non-obvious move: **slow down acquisitions, accelerate integration.** Most underperforming rollups are too quick to acquire more, too slow to integrate what they already have. Pausing acquisitions for 12 months to complete integration of the existing 8 companies usually delivers more EBITDA growth than acquiring 4 more.

## Recommendation skeleton

**Recommendation:** **Pause new acquisitions for 12 months.** Complete operational integration of existing 8 acquisitions: common dispatch software, centralized billing, fleet pooling, unified branding in 6 of 8 markets. Re-measure consolidated EBITDA at month 12. Resume acquisitions only after integration milestones met.

**Three supports:**

1. Integration is where rollup value is actually captured; acquisitions without integration produce no consolidated EBITDA lift.
2. 12 months is enough time to complete operational integration of 8 existing acquisitions.
3. The PE timeline is 5-7 years; pausing acquisitions for 12 months doesn't fundamentally compromise the exit timeline.

**One risk:** competitors may continue acquiring during our pause, locking up the best regional targets. Mitigate by maintaining the M&A pipeline relationships (LOIs, conversations) without closing deals, so we can re-accelerate when integration completes.

## What judges look for

- Did you identify **integration depth** as the dominant lever rather than acquisition pace?
- Did you propose a **pause to integrate** rather than acquiring through the problem?
- Did you score the integration explicitly by function?
- Did you recognize **customer retention + talent retention** as integration-execution risks?
