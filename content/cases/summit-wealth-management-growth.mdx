## The situation

Wealth-management AUM growth is a well-studied problem. The dominant industry growth driver is **advisor recruiting** — independent wealth firms compete intensely for established advisors with portable client books. M&A (acquiring smaller firms) is the secondary lever; organic client acquisition (through brand + marketing) is typically the smallest.

Doubling AUM in 5 years requires ~15% annual growth — well above industry average. Achievable but requires aggressive execution across multiple levers.

Three branches:

1. **Existing advisor productivity** — can current 200 advisors grow AUM organically (new client acquisition, share-of-wallet expansion)? Industry benchmark is 3-5% per year.
2. **Advisor recruiting** — net advisor growth at 10-20 advisors per year, with average book of $100-150M each, drives most of the AUM growth in this scale of firm.
3. **M&A + new segment** — acquiring small wealth firms (5-15 advisors each) at typically 2-3x revenue; launching mass-affluent digital advice as separate offering.

## Key analysis

The math:

- Existing advisor organic growth: 4% × $30B = $1.2B annually
- Recruiting net 15 advisors/year × $120M book = $1.8B annually
- M&A: 1-2 small firms per year × $1.5B AUM = $1.5-3B annually

Combined: ~$4.5-6B annual AUM growth. Over 5 years, that's $22-30B incremental, putting AUM at $52-60B. Achievable but tight on the doubled-AUM goal.

The mass-affluent digital channel is more speculative. The wealth industry has tried this multiple times (some succeed, many don't). It works when:

- The offering is genuinely different from the advisor-led model (lower cost, different positioning)
- The firm has digital execution capability (most wealth firms don't)
- The capital + patience is available for 3-5 years of investment before meaningful revenue

The non-obvious move: **focus the strategy on advisor recruiting + selective M&A; defer mass-affluent.** Recruiting is what Summit knows how to do; mass-affluent digital advice is a different business that risks distracting from the proven model.

## Recommendation skeleton

**Recommendation:** **Three-track growth plan over 5 years.** Track 1: advisor recruiting target of 18 net additions per year (improving from current 6 net additions); invest $50M in recruiting infrastructure + competitive packages. Track 2: M&A — acquire 1-2 small firms per year ($30-80M acquisition cost each, $300-800M cumulative deployed). Track 3: existing advisor productivity improvement (technology, platform, marketing support). Defer mass-affluent digital advice.

**Three supports:**

1. Recruiting + M&A combined produce realistic 15% AUM growth, matching the doubling target.
2. These two tracks build on Summit's existing capabilities rather than venturing into unfamiliar territory.
3. Mass-affluent digital advice is a $200-400M investment with uncertain payoff — deferral preserves capital + focus.

**One risk:** advisor recruiting in a tight market may not achieve 18/year if competitors raise their packages. Mitigate by building a strong recruiting brand (advisor testimonials, distinctive platform features) so price doesn't dominate every recruit decision.

## What judges look for

- Did you **decompose the AUM growth target** into specific flow sources?
- Did you size **advisor recruiting cost** (100-200% of TTM commissions) explicitly?
- Did you **defer mass-affluent digital advice** despite its trendy appeal?
- Did you tie each growth source to **specific operational requirements** (recruiting infrastructure, M&A capability)?
