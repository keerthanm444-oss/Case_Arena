## The situation

Price-driven competitive attacks are one of the most studied response problems in business strategy. The wrong response — matching prices reflexively — usually leads to industry margin destruction without restoring share. The right response depends on the attacker's structural position, the customer segments at risk, and the defender's differentiation capability.

Three branches:

1. **Attacker viability** — can the competitor sustain the lower pricing? If their unit economics don't support it, the price war is short-lived and matching is unnecessary.
2. **Customer segmentation** — which customers are actually price-shopping? SMB merchants vary in price sensitivity by size, transaction volume, ancillary needs.
3. **Differentiation options** — what value-added services do we have (capital advances, software, fraud protection) that justify a price premium?

## Key analysis

The diagnostic test: **disaggregate the 5% churn by customer profile.**

Three typical patterns:

- **Pure-price defectors** (mostly micro-merchants, single product type, sub-$100K processing volume): high elasticity. These customers are also the lowest-LTV in our base. Losing them is acceptable.
- **Bundled-value defectors** (mid-size merchants who use Tessera capital advances or software): churning these is more expensive — they're high-LTV.
- **Trial-then-back churners** (try the new entrant, find service quality lacking, return within 6 months): manageable noise.

If 70%+ of the churn is in the first category, the strategic damage is limited. The defection economics actually improve Tessera's average customer profile.

The non-obvious move: **selective price defense + bundled-value emphasis**, not blanket match. Match prices on a "loyalty pricing" tier for customers using 3+ Tessera services; let pure-price-shopper micro-merchants go.

This achieves two things: keeps high-LTV customers, and signals to the competitor that we're not in a price war — which removes their incentive to cut further.

## Recommendation skeleton

**Recommendation:** **Selective defense + bundle acceleration.** Action 1: introduce a "loyalty pricing" tier (10-15% discount for customers using 3+ services); deploy proactively to top 30% of SMB base. Action 2: accelerate bundle product roadmap (capital advances expansion, integrated POS) to deepen non-price differentiation. Action 3: walk away from pure-price competition for micro-merchants — let the bottom 10% of accounts go if they price-shop. Reduce SMB sales-team focus on these segments.

**Three supports:**

1. Loyalty tier protects high-LTV customers without triggering broader price war.
2. Bundle differentiation moves Tessera toward a value position that's harder to attack on price alone.
3. Letting micro-merchants go improves portfolio quality and avoids destroying industry margin.

**One risk:** if the new entrant achieves scale (10-15% share), their unit economics may strengthen enough to attack the bundled customer base. Mitigate by monitoring their growth + funding closely; be prepared to escalate response if attacker shows signs of cost-curve improvement.

## What judges look for

- Did you reject **reflexive price-matching** in favor of segmented analysis?
- Did you identify **customer-LTV by segment** to identify which churn is acceptable vs damaging?
- Did you propose a **loyalty-pricing mechanism** that defends without blanket discounting?
- Did you address the **competitor's viability** as a strategic input?
