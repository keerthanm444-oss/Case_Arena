## The situation

Heavy equipment digital services has been a defining strategic theme for the industry over the past 10 years. Caterpillar, John Deere, Komatsu have built substantial recurring-revenue businesses around telematics + predictive maintenance + fleet management. Late entrants face uphill battles because customer fleet data accumulates over years, and the leading platforms have multi-year head starts.

The strategic question for Titan: build, partner, or buy?

Three branches:

1. **Customer demand reality** — large fleet operators value digital services highly (5-10% of equipment cost in willingness to pay); small contractors much less. The market is concentrated.
2. **Competitive positioning** — leading competitors have 5-7 year head starts on data accumulation, dealer training, and customer onboarding. Late entry requires either differentiation or premium positioning.
3. **Execution requirements** — hardware (telematics retrofit + new equipment), software platform (data lake, ML models, customer-facing applications), services (dealer training, customer success).

## Key analysis

Realistic economics for a Titan-positioned digital-services play:

| Customer segment | Share of revenue | Willingness to pay | Adoption likelihood |
|------------------|------------------|--------------------|---------------------|
| Large fleet (100+ machines) | 35% | 5-8% of equipment cost | 70-80% |
| Mid-fleet (20-100 machines) | 30% | 2-5% | 40-60% |
| Small fleet (<20 machines) | 35% | 0-2% | 10-25% |

Weighted attach + revenue suggests a realistic digital services ceiling of $250-350M ARR at 3-5 year maturity — close to the CEO's $300M estimate but **only if execution is excellent.**

The non-obvious move: **focus exclusively on large fleet segment for years 1-3.**

Trying to serve all customer segments simultaneously dilutes resources and produces a worse product for the customers who actually pay. Large fleets have the budget, the in-house IT to integrate, and the operational sophistication to extract value from the platform.

Channel conflict is real — dealers earn from service revenue, and a centralized digital services offering competes with dealer service. Mitigation requires either revenue-sharing with dealers or careful positioning of digital services as additive (not replacing) dealer service.

## Recommendation skeleton

**Recommendation:** **Focused digital-services strategy targeting large fleet customers first.** Invest $80M over 3 years in data platform + software + customer success. Launch with 5-10 strategic large-fleet customers in year 1, scale to 100+ by year 3. Revenue target: $80M ARR by year 3, $300M ARR by year 6. Defer mid + small fleet expansion until large-fleet motion is profitable.

**Three supports:**

1. Large-fleet focus matches customer willingness-to-pay and adoption likelihood.
2. $80M investment over 3 years is achievable from existing cash flow without diluting equipment business margins.
3. Phased approach reduces risk of overcommitting to a digital business that may take longer to scale than expected.

**One risk:** competitors entrenched at large fleet customers may block our entry. Mitigate with differentiated offering (focus on specific job-site analytics that competitors haven't built) or strategic partnerships (embedding with construction management software).

## What judges look for

- Did you size **customer segment willingness-to-pay** to identify the real economic opportunity?
- Did you propose **focus on large fleet** rather than chasing all customer segments?
- Did you identify **dealer channel conflict** as a structural risk?
- Did you propose a **phased build** with realistic revenue ramp?
