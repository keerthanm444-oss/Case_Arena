## The situation

Veridian Telehealth has done well in a narrow lane: employer-sponsored mental-health benefits in the US. Customers are HR teams at mid-to-large companies; the product is therapist-matching, async messaging, and structured care plans. Margins are healthy because the employer pays per-seat regardless of utilization.

The board sees two motivations to expand internationally. First, **revenue diversification**: the US employer-benefits market is consolidating around a few large incumbents. Second, **talent access**: India has a large pool of English-speaking clinicians whose hourly cost is significantly below US rates.

## Branch 1 — is the market attractive?

Three sub-questions matter:

- **Segment.** "Urban middle class earning above a threshold" is functionally a different market from "rural population". You're not entering "India" — you're entering a specific income-and-geography segment. That distinction collapses the headline TAM from country-scale to perhaps the figure above (<LiveNumber compute="addressable_urban_pop" suffix="M" decimals={0} /> people).

- **Pay willingness.** Out-of-pocket mental-health spending in any emerging market is a small fraction of US levels. The employer-sponsored channel that drives Veridian's US business covers a much smaller share of urban workers in most emerging markets. So either you reduce the price point dramatically (lower ARPU above) or you find a different payer.

- **Competition.** A 5-Forces lens<Citation publisher="Competitive Strategy" title="Competitive Strategy: Techniques for Analyzing Industries and Competitors" authors={['Porter, Michael E.']} year={1980} /> typically shows: local incumbents have first-mover trust + distribution; international players usually struggle on localization; substitutes (in-person at low cost) are abundant; switching costs are negligible. Conclusion: competition is intense and differentiation is hard.

The headline read: **TAM is real but narrower than the headline figure**; competition is fierce; the regulatory backdrop is favorable in directionally most major emerging markets but specific rules require verification before commit.

## Branch 2 — can we win?

Veridian's US edge is employer-channel relationships + structured care plans for mental health. Of those, only the second translates directly. The first (employer-sponsored model) maps to a much smaller share of urban Indian workers.

The honest assessment: Veridian has weak distribution advantage, mediocre brand recognition, and zero local clinical network. Their strongest asset — the care-plan IP — is the part that's hardest for incumbents to copy.

This argues for an **asset-light, partner-heavy** entry: license the care-plan IP to a local platform, take a revenue share, avoid the brand-building + distribution lift. Build-from-scratch would be slow and expensive; acquiring an existing local player would be capital-intensive and require operational depth Veridian doesn't have.

## Branch 3 — how to enter?

Three options, ranked by capital efficiency:

1. **License + revenue share with a local incumbent.** Lowest capital, lowest control, fastest. Best for a "is this market real?" first foray.
2. **Joint venture with a local healthcare brand.** Medium capital, shared control, medium speed. Better when the local partner brings distribution + you bring product.
3. **Acquire a local player.** Highest capital, full control, slowest to integrate. Better only if you've already validated the market with options 1 or 2.

A staged approach — option 1, then 2, then maybe 3 — limits downside. The board's pressure for "a big India bet" should be resisted: the variance in our year-5 revenue estimate (above) tells us the responsible move is to learn first, then scale.

**Supporting points:**

2. Veridian's competitive edge — care-plan IP — is the asset most easily licensed and least diluted by partnership.
3. The cost of being wrong is bounded; the cost of being right and slow is the entire opportunity.

**Three go/no-go criteria at month 18:**

- ARPU above $25 in the partnership cohort.
- Active retention above 40% at month 6.
- Regulatory environment unchanged (especially on cross-border data flow).

## What judges look for

- Did you **size both scenarios** and call out the spread? The variance is the insight.
- Did you challenge "should we enter India" by asking what segment of India?
- Did you **separate** the entry decision from the entry mode?
- Did you propose a **staged commitment** with hard checkpoints, rather than a binary yes/no?
- Did you call out **bias risk** (CEO already wants this) and design a measurement plan that could falsify the bet?
