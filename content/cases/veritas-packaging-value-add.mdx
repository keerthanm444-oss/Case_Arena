## The situation

Corrugated packaging is one of the most-studied commodity industries. Industry structure (Five Forces) is unfavorable: many sellers, large buyers (CPG companies, e-commerce), commodity inputs, no real differentiation, high transport costs limiting geographic reach. Margin compression is structural unless individual companies find a way to differentiate.

Two paths exist: **scale** (consolidate to capture purchasing + manufacturing efficiency) and **differentiation** (invest in value-add capabilities customers will pay premium for). Most successful packaging companies pursue both selectively.

Three branches:

1. **Customer segmentation** — large CPG buyers (price-driven, scale-dependent) vs mid-market brands (design-sensitive, willing to pay premium for differentiation) vs e-commerce shippers (volume + cost focused).
2. **Competitive position** — scale-wise, who are we vs the top 3? Are we structurally cost-disadvantaged?
3. **Differentiation options** — sustainable materials (recyclable, compostable), smart packaging (RFID, QR, freshness sensors), design services (in-house creative team).

## Key analysis

The diagnostic insight: **different customer segments support different strategies.**

For large CPG buyers (~40% of Veritas revenue): pure cost game. Either Veritas matches the cost position of larger competitors (requires M&A consolidation) or loses share. M&A is the answer for this segment.

For mid-market brands (~35% of revenue): differentiation rewards. Brands launching new product lines value design support, sustainable substrates, fast turnaround. Willing to pay 15-25% premium. Differentiation investment is the answer.

For e-commerce shippers (~25% of revenue): volume + cost. Similar to large CPG.

So the answer is **both M&A and differentiation** — but targeted at different customer segments rather than as a blanket strategy.

The non-obvious move: **structure Veritas as two distinct business units**, one focused on scale-commodity (mostly automated, cost-driven), one focused on differentiated mid-market (sales force, design services, sustainability expertise). The current single-business structure forces compromises that hurt both.

## Recommendation skeleton

**Recommendation:** **Dual-strategy with internal restructuring.** M&A: acquire 1-2 mid-sized commodity-packaging players to gain scale in CPG + e-commerce segments ($800M-1.2B over 36 months). Differentiation: invest $200M in sustainable materials capability + design services targeting mid-market brands. Restructure into two business units to avoid compromise.

**Three supports:**

1. Customer segments have different economic drivers; one-size-fits-all strategy underperforms.
2. M&A captures scale-commodity opportunity where Veritas is structurally disadvantaged.
3. Differentiation investment captures mid-market premium where Veritas can win.

**One risk:** running two distinct business units requires management capability the company may not have. Mitigate by hiring a dedicated leader for the differentiated unit with packaging-innovation track record.

## What judges look for

- Did you reject **single-strategy** in favor of segment-specific approaches?
- Did you identify **mid-market as the differentiation opportunity** rather than treating all customers similarly?
- Did you propose **structural separation** (two business units) to avoid compromise?
- Did you size **M&A capital + differentiation investment** as distinct buckets?
