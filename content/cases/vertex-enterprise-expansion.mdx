## The situation

The "go upmarket" question is one of the most common strategic decisions in B2B SaaS. It's usually framed as "land bigger deals" — but the real question is whether the company has the capability + capital to execute a fundamentally different sales motion.

The CRO's skepticism is usually correct unless the product is genuinely enterprise-ready and the sales infrastructure is in place.

Three branches:

1. **Customer (enterprise buyer)** — committee-driven decisions, 9-18 month sales cycles, security + procurement reviews, multi-year contracts.
2. **Competition** — entrenched enterprise players have feature parity + integration depth; differentiation requires either substantial product investment or a focused vertical play.
3. **Company readiness** — product (SSO, SOC2 Type II, audit logs, role-based access), GTM (enterprise AE team, solution engineering, customer success), back-office (legal, security review responses).

## Key analysis

The diagnostic question: **what's the realistic deal economics, and what's the investment to get there?**

Typical enterprise SaaS deal economics:

- ACV: $200K-1M+ (vs $15K mid-market)
- Sales cycle: 9-15 months (vs 30-60 days mid-market)
- CAC: $150-400K per closed deal (vs $5-15K mid-market)
- Gross retention: 92-95% (vs 80-85% mid-market)
- Expansion within account: 130-150% NRR potential

The non-obvious finding: enterprise deals have *better* unit economics than mid-market deals, but only at scale. The first 10-20 enterprise customers usually lose money — the AE compensation + solution engineering + product investment isn't covered by the ACV until the motion is well-established.

The realistic 3-year path:

- Year 1: Build enterprise product features (~$15M product investment), hire 6-8 enterprise AEs (~$3M loaded cost), land 5-10 logos (likely losing money per deal in year 1)
- Year 2: Hit ~30 enterprise customers, deal economics start working
- Year 3: Enterprise becomes 30-40% of new ARR, motion is self-funding

## Recommendation skeleton

**Recommendation:** Pursue enterprise expansion on a **3-year dual-motion strategy**: maintain mid-market as the cash engine while building enterprise capability. Hire 4 enterprise AEs in year 1, invest $15M in product (SSO, SOC2, audit logs, on-prem option). Target 5-10 F500 logos by year 1 end. Re-evaluate at month 18 against specific metrics.

**Three supports:**

1. The unit economics of enterprise are better than mid-market — but only after the motion is established (years 2-3).
2. Dual-motion lets the mid-market business fund the enterprise build without forcing trade-offs.
3. Explicit milestones at month 18 (10 enterprise logos, 25% gross margin on enterprise deals) gate further investment.

**One risk:** enterprise AE talent is expensive and limited; over-hiring before product readiness wastes capital. Mitigate by phasing hiring to product milestone delivery.

## What judges look for

- Did you separate the **product readiness** problem from the **sales motion** problem?
- Did you size the **first 10-20 deals are loss-making** reality? Most candidates assume enterprise deals are immediately accretive.
- Did you propose **dual-motion** rather than treating it as binary?
- Did you specify **explicit milestone gates** rather than open-ended commitment?
