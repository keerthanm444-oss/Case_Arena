## The situation

Department-store decline is one of the most-studied retail patterns of the past 20 years. The structural drivers — mall traffic decline, category specialist competition, pure-play e-commerce, real-estate cost-base mismatch — are well understood. The candidate's job isn't to discover the drivers; it's to **design a credible response** given Westfield's specific assets.

The decisive question: can Westfield be a smaller, profitable department store, or is the only viable answer wind-down + real-estate monetization?

## Structuring

Three end-state options:

1. **Smaller, profitable department store** — close bottom-tier stores, focus on top-30 high-traffic locations, exit hopeless categories. Target: $1.5B revenue from 30 stores at 6% margin.
2. **Real-estate company with retail tenant** — convert to a REIT-like structure where the retail operation runs as a tenant of the real-estate entity. Common in private-equity playbooks for chains with valuable property.
3. **Orderly wind-down** — sell viable real estate, liquidate inventory, return capital to shareholders. Reasonable when no version of the retail business is viable.

## Key analysis

The diagnostic that decides between options: **the four-wall profitability cohort.**

Split the 75 stores into quartiles by four-wall operating margin:

- Top quartile (19 stores) — likely the only viable retail locations.
- Second quartile (19 stores) — break-even; could go either way.
- Bottom half (37 stores) — losing money on a four-wall basis. Cannot be saved by digital transformation; the unit economics don't work.

If the top quartile alone generates positive consolidated operating profit, Option 1 is viable. If not, Options 2 or 3.

In most Westfield-like scenarios, top-quartile revenue ÷ corporate overhead doesn't justify the corporate cost structure unless overhead is **dramatically** reduced (50%+ HQ cuts).

## Recommendation skeleton

**Recommendation:** Hybrid path. Close 30 bottom-tier stores in year 1. Operate the remaining 45 as a smaller, focused department store. Monetize real estate from the 30 closed locations (likely 60-70% of book value). Convert holding structure to enable separation of retail + real-estate at year 3 if retail still struggles.

**Three supports:**

1. The bottom-half stores can't be saved — their four-wall margin is too negative for any operational improvement to fix.
2. The 45 remaining stores can plausibly run at break-even or better if corporate overhead is cut proportionally (50%+ HQ reduction).
3. Real-estate monetization provides bridge capital for the transformation; without it, refinancing risk could force a worse outcome.

**One risk:** the closing-store labor and severance costs in year 1 are material — likely $200-400M depending on contracts. Mitigate by sequencing closures to allow some employees to transfer to remaining stores; targets WARN-Act compliance.

## What judges look for

- Did you recognize that **half the stores cannot be saved** and propose closure rather than treating each store as recoverable?
- Did you size **real-estate value** as a separate asset class? Many candidates ignore it.
- Did you propose a **hybrid path** rather than a binary "save vs wind down"?
- Did you identify **corporate overhead** as a critical lever? Cutting HQ proportionally is harder than cutting stores but more important.
