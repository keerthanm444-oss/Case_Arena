## The situation

US refining asset rationalization has been a defining theme of the past decade. California demand is declining ~2-3% YoY; East Coast refineries face similar pressures. The choices are limited: invest to keep running (uncertain demand), close (definite cost), or convert to biofuels (technology + feedstock risk).

Multiple refineries have already attempted biofuels conversion with mixed results — some successful (Marathon's Martinez), some abandoned.

Three branches:

1. **Continue refining economics** — demand trajectory, regulatory cost compliance, competitive position vs other refineries serving the market.
2. **Close economics** — decommissioning cost, environmental remediation, land monetization, asset disposal.
3. **Convert economics** — capex, feedstock supply, off-take contracts, regulatory incentive durability.

## Key analysis

Three-option NPV analysis:

**Option A — Invest $1.5B + continue:**
- Annual EBITDA: $300-500M (decreasing as demand falls)
- 10-year NPV: $1-2B (sensitive to demand decline rate)
- Risk: California gasoline demand could fall faster than expected

**Option B — Close + decommission:**
- One-time cost: $400-600M
- Land monetization: $200-300M potential
- 10-year NPV: -$200-400M
- Risk: stranded asset

**Option C — Convert to renewable diesel:**
- Capex: $800M-1.2B
- Annual EBITDA at scale: $200-400M (highly dependent on feedstock + credit values)
- 10-year NPV: $500M-1.5B
- Risk: feedstock supply, LCFS credit value, demand growth

The non-obvious finding: **conversion economics depend heavily on regulatory durability.** If LCFS + federal incentives compress in 5-10 years, renewable diesel margins collapse. The regulatory uncertainty is the largest single variable.

For Zenith specifically: conversion is attractive at current incentive levels but creates regulatory dependency. Closing is the simplest path but forfeits potential upside.

## Recommendation skeleton

**Recommendation:** **Convert with phased commitment.** Phase 1 (months 1-12): commit $200M for engineering + feedstock contracts; secure off-take agreements with major customers. Phase 2 (months 12-36): full conversion construction ($600M) IF feedstock + off-take secured. If conversion economics deteriorate during Phase 1, pivot to closure. Continue refining operations through conversion period.

**Three supports:**

1. Phased conversion limits capital at risk until critical contracts secured.
2. Renewable diesel economics at current incentive levels support conversion NPV exceeding both close + continue alternatives.
3. Phase 1 pivot-to-closure preserves optionality if conversion thesis weakens.

**One risk:** federal renewable fuel policy may change, compressing economics. Mitigate by securing long-term contracts (3-5+ year) with off-takers willing to bear policy risk.

## What judges look for

- Did you analyze **all three options on NPV basis** rather than choosing intuitively?
- Did you identify **regulatory incentive durability** as the key sensitivity for conversion?
- Did you propose **phased commitment** with go/no-go gate?
- Did you size **feedstock supply** as the operational binding constraint?
