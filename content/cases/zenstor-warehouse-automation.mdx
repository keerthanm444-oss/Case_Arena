## The situation

Warehouse automation economics have improved significantly with maturing technology + rising labor costs. The capex is still substantial — a fully-automated warehouse costs $50-200M — but payback periods have shortened from 7-10 years (historically) to 3-5 years for high-throughput facilities.

The strategic question: which warehouses should be automated, in what order, and to what extent (partial vs full automation)?

Three branches:

1. **Warehouse-level economics** — throughput, labor cost, contract length, automation payback by site.
2. **Automation tier** — partial (AMRs + sortation), substantial (ASRS + AMRs), full (goods-to-person, lights-out).
3. **Implementation sequencing** — which warehouses first, what change-management approach.

## Key analysis

Automation tiers + economics:

| Automation level | Capex per warehouse | Labor reduction | Payback (high throughput) |
|------------------|---------------------|-----------------|---------------------------|
| None (manual) | $0 | 0% | n/a |
| Partial (AMRs + sortation) | $15-30M | 25-40% | 2-3 years |
| Substantial (+ASRS) | $50-90M | 50-65% | 4-5 years |
| Full (goods-to-person) | $120-200M | 70-80% | 5-7 years |

The non-obvious finding: **partial automation has the best ROI for most warehouses.** Full automation requires very high throughput + long-term contracts to justify; partial automation works at moderate throughput + standard contracts.

For Zenstor's 60-warehouse network:
- Top 10 high-throughput sites with long contracts: substantial or full automation ($800M-1.5B total)
- Middle 30 sites: partial automation ($400-700M total)
- Bottom 20 small/variable sites: minimal automation or status quo

## Recommendation skeleton

**Recommendation:** **Tiered automation over 5 years.** Top 10 warehouses: substantial automation ($75M average; $750M total) — payback 4-5 years. Middle 30: partial automation ($20M average; $600M total) — payback 2-3 years. Bottom 20: targeted point-solutions (~$2M each; $40M). Total 5-year capex: $1.4B. Expected labor savings + capacity gains: $400-500M annually at maturity.

**Three supports:**

1. Tiered approach matches automation investment to warehouse-specific economics.
2. Partial automation captures most of the labor savings at much lower capex than full automation.
3. Phased rollout builds operational capability + change-management muscle progressively.

**One risk:** automation technology may advance faster than expected, making today's investment quickly obsolete. Mitigate by selecting modular systems (incremental upgrades possible) over fully-integrated bespoke systems.

## What judges look for

- Did you **tier warehouses by economics** rather than uniform automation?
- Did you identify **partial automation as highest ROI** for most sites?
- Did you propose **phased rollout** with capability building?
- Did you address **technology obsolescence risk** through modular system selection?
