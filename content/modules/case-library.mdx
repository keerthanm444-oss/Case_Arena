## What the case library is

The case library is the worked-case collection — every published case in this app, in one filterable index at <a href="/cases">/cases</a>. Each case follows the same structure: paraphrased problem statement, clarifying questions, an embedded interactive issue tree, reactive assumption chips, a recommendation skeleton, common mistakes, variants, and a "related cases" rail driven by the map graph.

The library is where the curriculum (M0–M8, M10) becomes concrete. Modules teach the moves; cases let you see those moves applied to a real problem.

> A case you've read passively is a case you don't yet know how to solve. The library is most useful when you treat each case as a problem to attempt before reading the solution — not a worked example to absorb passively.

## Three ways to find a case

The library can be filtered and explored three ways, optimized for different intents.

### 1. The index (`/cases`)

A filterable list. Multi-select by **industry**, **category**, **difficulty**, **framework**, **source**, and **solved/unsolved**. Free-text search across title + problem statement. The right tool when you have a specific shape in mind — e.g. "show me all retail profitability cases at standard difficulty" — and want a flat list to scan.

### 2. The case map (`/map`)

Three view modes over the same data:

- **Web view** — force-directed graph. Each case is a node; edges are weighted by shared frameworks, shared industry, and explicit `relatedCases` declarations from frontmatter. Use when you want to **discover related cases you didn't know about**.
- **Branch view** — hierarchical, grouped by industry → category. Use when you want to **see coverage** ("which industries have the most cases? where are the gaps?").
- **Matrix view** — framework × industry heatmap with cell counts. Use when you want to **find the intersection** ("which cases cover both market-entry and healthcare?").

All three views share the same filter state and a **Compare Tray** that holds up to 4 pinned cases for side-by-side comparison (industry, category, difficulty, frameworks, time, solved status).

### 3. From inside a case (`relatedCases` rail)

Every open case has a "Related cases" rail in the right sidebar, populated by the map graph's weighted neighbors (related-case=3 / shared-framework=2 / shared-industry=1). The simplest discovery path is often: read one case, then follow the strongest related edge to the next.

## How to read a worked case effectively

There's a wrong way and a right way. The wrong way:

1. Open the case
2. Read top-to-bottom including the recommendation
3. Nod thoughtfully
4. Close the case
5. Believe you've "studied" it

The right way:

1. Open the case
2. Read the problem statement + clarifying questions only
3. **Close the page** or paste the prompt into the <a href="/tools/issue-tree">issue tree tool</a>
4. Spend 10 minutes structuring + analyzing on your own
5. Return to the case page, compare your structure to the embedded issue tree
6. Read the recommendation
7. Note the gap between your version and the worked version
8. Pick one specific move from the worked version to try next time

The gap between "passively read 30 cases" and "actively worked 10 cases" is enormous. Cosentino emphasizes this distinction explicitly<Citation publisher="Case in Point" title="Case in Point — Complete Case Interview Preparation" authors={['Cosentino, Marc']} year={2023} /> and it shows up in nearly every observed prep cycle: active workers progress 3-5× faster than passive readers per case opened.

<Callout tone="tip" title="The 10-minute discipline">
  If you only have 10 minutes for a case, spend it structuring + recommending in your head. Don't read the solution. The structuring is what compounds; reading solutions without trying first feels productive but barely is.
</Callout>

## A "first 5 cases" path

If you've finished the modules and want a starter set, here's a sequence that aligns with the curriculum:

### Case 1 — pair with M1 (Structuring)

Pick any **introductory profitability case** (filter: difficulty=intro, category=profitability). The first case is about getting comfortable with the structuring move, not about getting the right answer. <CaseLink slug="acme-coffee-decline">Acme Coffee</CaseLink> is the default starter — pure profitability, retail context, ~35 min.

**Focus:** can you build a defensible profit tree in under 4 minutes, before reading the solution?

### Case 2 — pair with M2 (Analysis)

Pick a case with a **sizing or quant-heavy** component (filter: tags=sizing OR frameworks=market-sizing). The goal is to drive the actual numbers with the assumption chips, not just nod at them.

**Focus:** which two assumptions drive the answer most? Run them through the <a href="/tools/sizing">sizing calculator</a> with sensitivity on.

### Case 3 — pair with M3 (Synthesis)

Pick a case with a clear recommendation section (any case in the library; difficulty=standard or above). The goal isn't to redo the analysis — it's to **write your own synthesis** before reading the case's version.

**Focus:** can you produce a recommendation + 3 supports + 1 risk in under 90 seconds of writing, then compare to the case's recommendation?

### Case 4 — pair with M4 (Communication)

Pick any standard-difficulty case. Deliver the synthesis from Case 3 **out loud** with the <a href="/tools/timer">practice timer</a> set to 8 minutes (the canonical presentation pace from M4).

**Focus:** does your verbal delivery match the answer-first discipline of the worked case's recommendation? Record yourself; identify your specific delivery tells.

### Case 5 — pair with M7 (Competitions)

Pick a final-round difficulty case — explicitly the hardest tier. Run it under realistic competition conditions: 60 minutes prep, 8 minutes presentation, 6 minutes Q&A from a partner who's read the case ahead of time.

**Focus:** can you sustain the M0–M4 disciplines under sustained pressure? This case is a stress test for whether the curriculum has compounded into real skill.

By case 5, you've exercised the full M0–M7 stack at least once each in a structured way. Repeat with new cases to build pattern recognition; the marginal value of each subsequent case grows once the moves are familiar.

## Studying solved vs unsolved cases

Each case has a `solved` flag in its frontmatter. Solved cases have a worked recommendation; unsolved cases have the structure (problem, clarifying questions, framework) but leave the recommendation as an exercise.

When to read **solved** cases:
- Early in prep, when you don't yet know what good looks like
- After attempting a similar problem to compare
- When studying a specific move (e.g. "how does a strong M&A recommendation read?")

When to attempt **unsolved** cases:
- Mid-prep, when you have the moves but need pattern fluency
- In timed practice (no worked answer to bias toward)
- With a mock partner — they can see your work without a built-in answer to compare against

A mature prep cycle uses both: maybe 60% solved (for studying patterns), 40% unsolved (for practicing without anchors).

## Coverage gaps

The library doesn't have full uniform coverage. Some industries (retail, healthcare) are over-represented relative to others (energy, public sector) because the source material has more retail + healthcare cases. The <a href="/map">matrix view</a> shows the gaps visually.

If you have a specific industry interest and the library is thin there, the right move is usually:

1. **Find a real published case from that industry** in one of the canonical sources (HBS, Ivey, McKinsey, etc.).
2. **Practice with it as if it were in the library** — read the prompt, close the source, structure on your own.
3. **Map the structure to one of the canonical frameworks** even if the source's framework is different — most cases reduce to profitability, market-entry, M&A, or sizing.

The library exists to provide variety + interactive practice. Real published sources are the deeper material; the library is the curated entry point.

## How cases get added

Each case in this library is a `.mdx` file in `content/cases/`. The frontmatter includes the source citation (paraphrased prompts, never verbatim — see <a href="/modules/orientation">M0</a> on the citation contract). The build linter blocks production deploys that contain unverified citations.

If you're contributing a case: see [`docs/AUTHORING.md`](https://github.com/case-arena/case-arena/blob/main/docs/AUTHORING.md) for the full schema and citation rules. The contract is identical for first-party and contributor content — no fabrication, every prompt paraphrased, every quote ≤ 15 words.

## Common library-use failure modes

Five patterns that recur in observed library usage:

1. **Reading without attempting.** Cases read passively don't compound. Always attempt the structure before reading the solution.

2. **Skipping the clarifying questions.** The clarifying-questions section is where strong candidates train their probing instincts. Don't skip it.

3. **Ignoring the related-cases rail.** The graph is built to make discovery cheap. Use it.

4. **Cherry-picking only the easy cases.** Difficulty progression matters. After 5 cases at intro level, force yourself to attempt one at standard. Discomfort is the signal of growth.

5. **Not tracking what you've worked.** The dashboard records visited pages; review them weekly to see whether you're varying by industry + framework or grooving the same shape.
