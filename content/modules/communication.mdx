## Why delivery is its own skill

You can have the right structure (M1), defensible analysis (M2), and a clean synthesis (M3) — and still lose the room. Delivery is the layer that translates internal coherence into external persuasion. It's the difference between "I have the right answer" and "the judges remember the right answer."

> A great synthesis delivered hesitantly reads as uncertain. A weak synthesis delivered confidently reads as a missed opportunity. Both lose. The goal is **good synthesis × confident delivery**.

The good news: delivery is mostly mechanics, learnable from practice. The five sections below cover what to practice, in roughly the order it matters.

## Slide design as visual structure

In a presentation case, slides are the spine. Each slide should do one job, signaled by an action title (M3) and supported by minimal visual evidence.

The slide-design principles that recur across consulting reference texts:<Citation publisher="The Pyramid Principle" title="The Pyramid Principle: Logic in Writing and Thinking" authors={['Minto, Barbara']} year={2009} />

1. **One claim per slide.** If you can't compress the slide's argument into a single sentence (its action title), the slide is doing too much. Split it.

2. **Action title at top.** "Revenue gains share at twice market rate" beats "Revenue trend". The reader should be able to skim only the titles and follow the argument. The <a href="/tools/slide-critique">slide critique muscle</a> checks for this.

3. **Visual evidence in the body.** Chart, table, or two bullets — pick one. Stuffing all three onto a slide signals you didn't decide what mattered.

4. **Strip everything else.** No clip art, no decorative borders, no "transition" effects. Every visual element should earn its place by serving the claim.

5. **Numbers in tables; trends in charts.** A chart of three quarters is just a colored table. A table of 30 quarters is illegible — chart it.

### The 6-slide structure that wins most rounds

For an 8-minute presentation, this 6-slide skeleton works for ~80% of cases:

1. **Recommendation** — the answer, one slide.
2. **Context** — what we were asked and why it matters.
3. **Support 1** — opportunity / market / quantification.
4. **Support 2** — capability / fit / why we can win.
5. **Support 3** — risk / mitigation / what could go wrong.
6. **Next steps** — what we'd do in the first 90 days.

That's it. Six action-titled slides, one claim each, 80 seconds per slide.

<Callout tone="tip" title="The cover-slide trap">
  Many candidates use slide 1 as a "title slide" with the case name + logo. Don't. Slide 1 is your most valuable real estate — put the recommendation on it. The case name belongs in the deck filename or the corner footer.
</Callout>

## Narrative arc across an 8-minute presentation

A presentation is a story, not a document read aloud. The arc that works:

**Open with the recommendation.** First sentence: "Our recommendation is to enter Brazil in Q3 via JV." Don't bury it. The judges already know the case — they want to know what you'd do.

**State the supports as numbered claims.** "Three reasons: opportunity, capability, risk." This signals to listeners how many things you'll cover; they'll track better.

**Walk each support with evidence + implication.** The body of the talk is just three "evidence → implication" loops. Each loop is 60–90 seconds.

**Surface the biggest risk explicitly.** "The single thing that could break this recommendation is X. Here's how we'd watch for it." Showing you've thought about failure modes signals strength, not weakness.

**Close with action.** "Three things we'd do in the first 90 days: A, B, C." The last sentence the room hears is the call to action.

That structure mirrors the M3 Pyramid: top → three supports → close. The visual deck mirrors it slide-for-slide. The verbal narration mirrors it. Coherence across all three layers (synthesis, deck, talk) is what makes a presentation feel inevitable rather than assembled.

## Physical presence

The tactical mechanics that almost never get practiced:

- **Stand still.** Pacing reads as nervous. Plant your feet at the start of each support; move only to the next slide's anchor.
- **Hands visible.** Pockets read as defensive; behind your back reads as stiff. Keep them in front of your body, at rib height.
- **Eye contact for whole sentences.** Pick one judge, deliver one full thought to them, then move to the next. Not 6-word fragments scanning across.
- **Pause before the close.** When you reach "in summary" or "our recommendation", pause for 1 full beat first. The pause says "what's coming next matters."
- **No filler words during pauses.** Silence reads as confident; "um", "so", "basically" read as hedging.

These are mechanical. They take ~20 minutes of recorded practice to identify your specific tells, then a few rounds of self-correction. Most candidates never do this work; doing it is a real edge.

## Handling Q&A

Q&A is half the score in most case-competition rubrics, and it's the part candidates practice least. Three moves that handle ~90% of questions:

### Move 1 — Restate the question

Before answering, restate what you heard: "If I understood — you're asking whether our $5M cap is the right number versus a larger commitment?" Two benefits: confirms you heard correctly, buys you 3 seconds to think.

### Move 2 — Lead with the answer, then justify

Same Pyramid discipline as the synthesis. "Yes, the $5M cap is the right number. Three reasons…" Not "let me walk through the analysis again."

### Move 3 — Bound your confidence

If you don't know, say so cleanly. "I don't have data on that, but my intuition is X because Y. The way I'd verify is Z." That's an honest non-answer with structure; it preserves credibility better than guessing.

<Callout tone="warning" title="The killer Q&A tell">
  When judges sense bluffing, they double down with follow-ups specifically designed to expose it. The path out is to call it: "I'm extrapolating here — I don't have hard data on that specific point." That single sentence resets the dynamic. Judges respect epistemic honesty far more than confident wrongness.
</Callout>

## Recovery moves when things go wrong

Things will go wrong. The seven most common failure modes and the recovery move for each:

1. **You forget a number mid-sentence.** Recover: "I'm going to pull that from the appendix — the order of magnitude is X." Don't freeze. Order of magnitude is almost always defensible from memory.

2. **A judge asks something you didn't plan for.** Recover: Use the bounding-confidence move above. Don't pretend to know.

3. **Your analysis turns out wrong mid-presentation.** Recover: "I want to correct what I said earlier — on further thought, the figure is X, not Y. The recommendation still holds because…" Calling your own error reads as strength.

4. **You're 90 seconds behind schedule.** Recover: Skip the next support entirely and pivot to the close. "In the interest of time, I'll combine supports 2 and 3 — both are about capability fit." Better than racing through three rushed supports.

5. **A judge interrupts mid-recommendation.** Recover: Answer the interruption fully, then explicitly return: "To the original recommendation — three supports, the first is…" Don't let the interruption derail the spine.

6. **You realize you misunderstood the prompt.** Recover: This is the worst failure. Say so explicitly, propose what you'd do with five more minutes, and ask if you can pivot. Judges sometimes allow this; even when they don't, the move shows good faith.

7. **Tech fails mid-presentation.** Recover: Continue without the deck. The structure should be memorized; if it isn't, that's a preparation problem the recovery can't fix.

## Pre-flight ritual

The five-minute pre-flight that takes a presentation from "competent" to "polished":

1. **Read the recommendation aloud.** If it doesn't sound right in your own voice, fix it before it's in front of judges.
2. **Time the first slide.** If your opening runs over 90 seconds, you'll be rushed throughout. Tighten before the room.
3. **Predict the first three questions.** Have a one-sentence answer for each. The judges will ask at least one.
4. **Walk through the worst-case scenario.** What's the question you don't want? Rehearse the answer once.
5. **Check the room.** Where you'll stand, where the screen is, where the timer is. Familiarity removes one variable.

This ritual takes <em>five</em> minutes and most candidates skip it entirely. Doing it consistently is an edge.

## Common failure modes

Five mistakes that recur across delivery:

1. **Reading the slide aloud.** The audience can read faster than you can speak. Use the slide as evidence; don't narrate it.
2. **Apologizing.** "Sorry, I'm a bit nervous" or "I don't have great data here" undercuts the next 3 minutes. Cut the apology; commit.
3. **Hedging with the body.** Shoulders forward, eyes down, voice trailing. Posture is half the message; if it says uncertain, the words don't recover.
4. **Treating Q&A as a defense.** Approach Q&A as the most useful part — judges are telling you exactly what they want addressed. Engage, don't deflect.
5. **No closing move.** Trailing off at the end ("…so yeah, that's it") loses the close. Plan the last sentence and deliver it deliberately.

## Self-test

Find a 6-minute window. Pull up the <CaseLink slug="acme-coffee-decline">Acme Coffee case</CaseLink>. Without writing anything down, deliver the recommendation aloud — Pyramid shape, three supports, one risk, close on action. Record yourself if you can.

Then watch the recording and check:

- Did you lead with the answer in the first sentence?
- Were your three supports on three different dimensions?
- Did you pause before the close?
- Did you have any filler words?
- Did your last sentence land?

If three of those five are yes, you've internalized M4. Fix the others with targeted practice.
