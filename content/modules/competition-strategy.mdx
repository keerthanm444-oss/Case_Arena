## Why competitions need their own strategy

By M7 you understand competition formats. M10 specializes the rest of the curriculum for the competition setting specifically — the moves that win when **four teams are presenting to a panel within the same hour**, not when one candidate is being evaluated by one interviewer.

The shift is decisive. Interview psychology (M6) is about whether the judge would hire you. **Competition psychology is about whether the panel ranks you above three other teams they just watched**. Different problem, different answer.

> A competition isn't won by being objectively correct. It's won by being **the most memorable defensible team in the room**. Most teams optimize only for "correct"; the gap is where the wins happen.

## How panels actually score

Most competition rubrics list 4-6 categories: typically some combination of problem definition, analysis, recommendation, communication, Q&A, teamwork. Each is rated 1-10. The team's score is a weighted sum.

That's the explicit process. The implicit process is different and matters more:

1. **The panel forms a gestalt impression in the first 90 seconds.** That impression sets a prior that the rubric scores then adjust modestly. Strong opens compound throughout the presentation.

2. **One judge typically anchors the others.** In a 4-judge panel, scores correlate ~0.7. The most confident scorer pulls the others. Strong teams identify the anchor judge during Q&A and engage them.

3. **The Q&A often dominates the analysis score.** The panel says they score Analysis 1-10 based on the model; they actually score it 1-10 based on how the team **defends** the model under pressure. A clean model with shaky defense scores below a slightly-rougher model with confident defense.

4. **Recency effects matter.** The last team to present has a 5-10% scoring advantage on average. There's nothing you can do about your slot, but if you have a choice, take a late one.

## Reading the rubric

If the rubric is published before the event — which it usually is — read it once carefully, then audit your deck against it explicitly. Three questions per category:

1. **What evidence in our deliverable maps to this category?** If you can't point at a specific slide for "Analysis", you'll score poorly there regardless of how rigorous the underlying work was.
2. **Is the evidence prominent enough that a judge skimming will see it?** Buried evidence scores lower than weaker evidence prominently placed.
3. **If a category is weighted 2x, are we spending 2x the slide real estate on it?** Most teams produce balanced decks against unbalanced rubrics.

The simple discipline of mapping deck → rubric pre-submission catches most score-leaking. It costs 30 minutes and recovers 10-20% of available points.

<Callout tone="tip" title="The rubric-weighted opening">
  If the rubric weights "Recommendation" highest (it usually does), your first 30 seconds should be the recommendation in its strongest form. If "Analysis" is weighted highest, the first 30 seconds should be the headline analytical insight. Match the open to the weighting.
</Callout>

## The 90-second open

The first 90 seconds of your presentation set the prior. Practice the open to mechanical perfection.

A strong open has four moves, in this order:

1. **Frame the question** in one sentence. "We were asked whether [client] should [decision]."
2. **State the recommendation** in one sentence. "Our recommendation is to [action], because [one-phrase reason]."
3. **Preview the supports.** "We'll show three reasons: opportunity, capability, risk."
4. **Bridge to the deck.** "Let's start with the opportunity."

Total: 90 seconds. Practiced enough that you could deliver it under any conditions — sleep-deprived, mid-flu, interrupted by a fire alarm.

The 90-second open is the single most-rehearsed thing for competition-winning teams. It's the spot where over-rehearsal pays off; everywhere else, the line between rehearsed and stiff matters more.

## Panel Q&A — different from interview Q&A

A panel asks Q&A differently than a single interviewer. Three patterns:

### Pattern 1 — judges signal to each other

Watch the panel's body language as one judge asks a question. If two others lean in, that question is from the anchor judge — answer it with extra care. If the others look away or check papers, the question is lower-stakes — answer cleanly but don't over-invest.

### Pattern 2 — judges share questions through follow-ups

A senior judge will sometimes ask "and to build on what [other judge] just asked…" — that's the panel coordinating. Your answer should acknowledge both questions, not just the most recent one. "To address both questions together — first, [first judge's frame]; second, [latest judge's specific point]."

### Pattern 3 — silence is a probe

Panels deliberately leave silence after an answer to see if you fill it. The right move is the same as in interview Q&A: **don't fill it**. Sit. The panel will move on when they're ready.

### Three Q&A moves panels reward

- **Anticipating a follow-up before it's asked.** "You'll likely ask about regulatory risk — that's the single biggest threat to this recommendation. Here's how we'd handle it." Panels love this; it signals you've thought past the surface.

- **Naming a weakness in your own work proactively.** "Our adoption curve assumption is the weakest part of our model — we used [X] but the range is [Y]." Calling your own weakness builds credibility; waiting for the panel to find it loses points.

- **Pushing back politely when wrong.** If a judge asserts something incorrect, **don't agree to keep the peace**. "I'd actually disagree with that — here's why." Panels respect well-defended disagreement far more than nodded agreement.

## Differentiating from other teams

If you're in a strong field, several teams will reach the same recommendation. Differentiation matters. Three moves that reliably create distance:

### Move 1 — explicitly reject the "obvious" answer

If the obvious recommendation is "enter the market", strong teams that recommend entering still acknowledge it: "the obvious answer is to enter. We agree, but we want to highlight that the case for entry rests on three non-obvious factors…" This signals you considered the counter-case and didn't just default to the consensus.

### Move 2 — find one non-obvious risk

Every team will list the obvious risks (competition, regulation, capital). Strong teams find one risk the others won't — usually by thinking through the second-order effects of the recommendation (what does this signal to other players? how do they react?). A non-obvious risk is memorable; obvious risks read as filler.

### Move 3 — propose a specific, testable next step

"We recommend X. To validate before committing, we'd run [specific 30-day test] with success criteria [Y] and [Z]." A specific test signals operational seriousness; vague "next steps" signal academic exercise.

## Pre-presentation team coordination

In the 15 minutes before you present, the team should align on five things:

1. **Who delivers the open?** Usually the strongest presenter; ideally the synthesizer.
2. **Who delivers each section?** Map the deck to specific speakers.
3. **What's the "second story"?** If the panel pushes back on the main recommendation, what's the alternative path? Don't be caught switching frameworks live.
4. **What questions are we expecting?** List the top 5 questions and the assigned answerer for each.
5. **What's the redirect signal?** If a teammate is struggling mid-answer, what's the agreed signal for another teammate to step in?

Teams that skip this 15-minute coordination lose 10-15% of points to avoidable coordination errors (two teammates talking over each other, no one answering a hard question, repetition of points already covered).

## Handling losses

You will lose competitions. Often. Even strong teams win <40% of the competitions they enter. The lessons matter; the rumination doesn't.

After a loss, run a structured debrief within 24 hours:

1. **What did the winning team do that we didn't?** Be specific. Not "they were better" but "their analysis slide had a sensitivity tornado; ours didn't."
2. **What rubric category did we score lowest on?** If you can't get the scorecard, infer it from the judges' Q&A — the categories they probed hardest are usually the categories they scored you lowest on.
3. **What one thing would we do differently?** Resist the urge to list five things. One specific, learnable change.
4. **Is it worth re-entering this competition next year?** Sometimes yes, sometimes no. Format-fit matters more than persistence.

Spiral-then-resolve is the operating mode. Spiral for 24 hours, name the lesson, move on.

## Common failure modes

Five patterns that recur across competition losses:

1. **Generic recommendations.** "Increase marketing spend" is what every team recommends. The winning team recommended "increase marketing spend in channel X by 40%, redirected from channels Y and Z, with a specific 90-day test in 3 markets". Specificity is the differentiator.

2. **Beautiful deck, mediocre content.** Competition judges discount visual polish heavily when the underlying analysis is thin. Spend the design time after the analysis is sound, not before.

3. **No Q&A rehearsal.** A team that drilled the 15-minute presentation but skipped the 10-minute Q&A is signaling exactly where to attack. Judges sense it within 60 seconds.

4. **Reading from notes.** Even visible-but-not-read notes signal you don't own the material. If you can't deliver without notes, you don't know the case well enough yet.

5. **Conceding to every judge pushback.** Strong teams disagree with at least one judge during Q&A. The disagreement signals you've thought independently rather than absorbed what the panel wanted to hear.

## Self-test

Pick a recent competition you watched, judged, or competed in. Without re-watching the recordings, answer:

1. What were the explicit rubric categories? What were they weighted?
2. Which team had the strongest 90-second open? What made it work?
3. Where did the Q&A go best for each team? Where did it go worst?
4. What did the winning team do that others didn't?
5. If you had been in the room, what would your differentiated move have been?

If you can answer all five about a competition you watched, you've internalized M10. If you can't, watch one more — the patterns become obvious once you know what to look for.
