## Why competitions are different from interviews

By M6, you have the skill stack for case interviews. **Case competitions** share the foundation but differ in three structural ways:

1. **They're team events.** 3-4 person teams instead of solo. Coordination + role allocation matter as much as individual skill.
2. **They have longer prep windows.** Hours to weeks instead of minutes. The work shifts from "perform well in the room" to "produce a high-quality deliverable, then defend it".
3. **They're judged by panels, not individuals.** 3-5 judges with mixed backgrounds means you're optimizing for the median view, not maximizing for any single judge's preferences.

These three differences cascade into everything: the right preparation routine, the right team composition, the right risk profile in your recommendation. M7 maps the terrain.

## The four canonical competition formats

Most case competitions fit one of four shapes. Knowing the shape tells you what to optimize for.

### Format 1 — short-prep crit case (24–72 hour prep)

Teams receive a case prompt, have 24 to 72 hours to research, build a deck, and deliver an 8–15 minute presentation to judges. Q&A typically 5–10 minutes.

**What it tests:**
- Speed of structuring under uncertainty
- Team coordination under sleep deprivation
- Slide craft (the deck is half the score)
- Synthesis discipline when you've over-analyzed

**Strategic implication:** the team that locks the recommendation by hour 8 of a 24-hour case usually wins. Teams still debating direction at hour 18 produce decks that show the indecision. Decide early, then defend.

### Format 2 — long-prep crit case (1–4 week prep)

Teams receive the case weeks in advance, present at an event. Deliverables typically include a full deck, an executive summary, and sometimes a financial model. Presentation 15–30 minutes; Q&A 10–20.

**What it tests:**
- Depth of research and primary data gathering
- Financial modeling rigor
- Sustained team coordination over weeks
- The ability to make the deck feel new on presentation day despite weeks of work

**Strategic implication:** scope discipline matters most. Teams that try to investigate everything end up communicating nothing. Pick three deep threads early, develop them thoroughly, ignore everything else.

### Format 3 — live case with rounds

Multiple elimination rounds, each with shorter prep (sometimes 60–90 minutes). Teams advance to semifinals + finals. Final round is often a fresh prompt.

**What it tests:**
- Consistency across rounds (you can't peak once)
- Range — the prompt domains differ between rounds
- Resilience after a weak round
- Quick team recalibration when the judge feedback signals what's wanted

**Strategic implication:** preserve energy across rounds. Sleep is undervalued; doing the bare-minimum prep for the night-before-finals beats a sleep-deprived "extra hour of work" almost every time.

### Format 4 — research-driven competition

The prompt is open-ended (e.g. "propose a solution to a real social issue"). Teams have weeks to months. Output is a written report + presentation. Judging often weighs research quality + originality alongside structure.

**What it tests:**
- Genuine research skill
- Original synthesis from primary data
- Long-form writing quality
- Defensibility in adversarial Q&A from subject-matter experts

**Strategic implication:** the winning teams here usually have an actual subject-matter expert on board, not just generalist case-prep skill. Recruit accordingly.

## What each round actually tests

For multi-round competitions, the rounds are not the same case repeated four times. Each round is testing something different. Map the round to its test:

### Round 1 — qualifier

**Test:** can you produce a competent, complete recommendation under time pressure? Quality bar is "no obvious errors", not "outstanding insight".

**Strategy:** prioritize completeness over depth. A 6-slide deck that hits all six required moves cleanly beats a brilliant 3-slide deck that skipped the financial model. Don't risk the unconventional move yet.

### Round 2 — quarter/semifinal

**Test:** can you do round 1's work, plus produce one genuinely interesting insight? The pure-competence teams get filtered out here.

**Strategy:** find one move that surprises the judges in a defensible direction. A novel framing, a sharp sensitivity that reveals a counter-intuitive conclusion, a recommendation that explicitly considers and rejects the "obvious" answer.

### Round 3 — final

**Test:** under maximum pressure (often with more senior judges + larger audience), can you sustain the quality of the earlier rounds? The pure-novelty teams that peaked in semis usually crack here.

**Strategy:** stay with what worked. Resist the urge to do something dramatically different in finals. The team that delivers their semifinal performance — but with sharper Q&A — wins.

## Team composition

A strong 4-person team has these roles, often overlapping:

- **The synthesizer.** Writes the action titles, holds the through-line, decides what stays in the deck. Often the team lead.
- **The quant.** Builds the financial model, runs the sensitivities, owns the data. Spotted by being the one everyone asks "is this number right?".
- **The researcher.** Finds the cited evidence, pulls the comps, owns the appendix. Usually the most patient team member.
- **The presenter.** Delivers the talk in the room. Sometimes the same person as the synthesizer; sometimes deliberately a different voice for the room.

Two anti-patterns:

- **All synthesizers, no quant.** The team produces beautiful slides with shaky numbers. Fails in Q&A.
- **All researchers, no presenter.** The team produces a thorough analysis no one can deliver compellingly. Fails on stage.

If your team is missing a role, **explicitly assign it to someone** who'll grow into it for this competition. Better than leaving the role unfilled.

## How to pick competitions

The "should I enter this" calculus, in order of weight:

1. **Format match.** Does this competition's format play to skills you currently have or want to build? A short-prep team competition won't develop deep financial modeling; a long-prep finance one won't develop quick structuring. Pick deliberately.

2. **Team match.** Do you have 3 people who'll show up reliably for the prep window? A weeks-long competition with an unreliable teammate is worse than not entering. Solo-equivalent reliability matters more than individual brilliance.

3. **Realistic skill gap.** If your team has never won a competition, target ones where the median entrant has roughly your skill level. Stretching is good; entering with a 3× skill gap demoralizes the team.

4. **Career signal.** Some competitions matter for hiring (the firm-sponsored ones). Most don't. Be honest about whether you're entering for skill-building or signaling — both are valid, but they steer you toward different competitions.

5. **Sunk cost of travel + opportunity cost of time.** A 4-day international event with a small prize that you'll travel to costs a real week of your life. Worth it sometimes; not always.

## Common failure modes for competition teams

Five patterns that recur across observed team performances:

1. **Late convergence.** Teams that still debate the recommendation at hour 18 of a 24-hour case have already lost. Lock the recommendation by hour 8.

2. **Over-investing in the deck.** The slides matter, but spending hour 22 making the title slide beautiful while the financial model has a known error is misallocation. Fix the substance first.

3. **No Q&A rehearsal.** Teams that drill the 12-minute presentation but skip the 10-minute Q&A get exposed in the part of the event that's half the score.

4. **Role drift.** Two team members both trying to be the synthesizer creates conflict over the through-line. Assign roles early; honor them.

5. **Solo-prep mentality.** Teams that have everyone work alone for 20 hours then assemble at hour 22 lose to teams that synced every 3 hours. Coordination overhead is real but the cost of not paying it is higher.

## Self-test

Pick one real competition you're considering or have been adjacent to. Answer the four "what you actually need to know" questions about it (team size + eligibility, prep window + deliverable, round structure, rubric) from the published source materials. If you can't find clear answers for any of them, can you commit?

Then ask: if your team won this, what skill would the win demonstrate? If you can't name a specific skill, the prestige is doing the work — that's a worse bet than picking a competition that builds something specific.
