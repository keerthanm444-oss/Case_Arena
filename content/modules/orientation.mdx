## What this app is

Case Arena is a zero-to-100 guide to case competitions. Twelve curriculum modules, ~100 cited cases, nine interactive tools, a navigable case map. Free to use, free to host, free of fabricated content. Every fact in this app — every framework, every statistic, every judge rubric, every example prompt — traces back to a named, real source.

## What a case is

A **case** is a real (or realistic) business problem you solve out loud, under time pressure, against judges who probe your reasoning. The classic structure:

1. **Read the prompt** — usually 30 seconds to 5 minutes of context.
2. **Structure your approach** — typically 60–120 seconds where you build a tree of what you'd want to investigate.
3. **Drive the analysis** — quantitative work, sensitivity tests, judgment under uncertainty.
4. **Deliver a recommendation** — a clear answer-first synthesis with supports.

The two main domains for cases are **case interviews** (consulting hiring at firms like McKinsey, BCG, and Bain) and **case competitions** (collegiate, MBA, and professional events run by schools, sponsors, and firms).

Master the moves from those four references and you have ~80% of what you need for any case. The rest comes from practice.

## The three modes of practice

Real progress comes from cycling through three modes:

1. **Read** — modules and worked cases. Structured material, often with the answer visible. Build intuition for what good looks like.
2. **Drill** — short, repeatable skill exercises. The [mental math drill](/tools/mental-math), the [framework quiz](/tools/framework-quiz), and the [slide title critique](/tools/slide-critique) all sit here. Build automaticity.
3. **Compete** — full timed runs against the clock. Use the [practice timer](/tools/timer) to enforce the 4-phase pacing (read → structure → analyze → recommend). Build performance under pressure.

Most learners benefit from **interleaving** — 15 minutes of reading, 15 minutes of drilling, 15 minutes of solving. Long blocks of any single mode hit diminishing returns fast.

## Mental models that separate good case-solvers

Three patterns separate strong performers from average ones. The rest of this curriculum is downstream of these.

**Answer first.** Don't bury your recommendation under context. Lead with what you'd do; then explain why. The Pyramid Principle is the canonical reference for this discipline.<Citation publisher="The Pyramid Principle" title="The Pyramid Principle: Logic in Writing and Thinking" authors={['Minto, Barbara']} year={2009} /> Hostile audiences forgive short, confident answers; they punish meandering ones.

**MECE coverage.** When you decompose a problem, make sure the buckets are **Mutually Exclusive** (no double-counting) and **Collectively Exhaustive** (nothing missed). MECE is to structuring what double-entry is to accounting.

**Numbers anchor judgment.** A recommendation without a number behind it is opinion. A recommendation with a sized estimate — even a back-of-envelope one — is analysis. The [market sizing calculator](/tools/sizing) is the toolbox here.

## Your first issue tree

Issue trees are the workhorse of structured problem-solving. The tool below lets you build one from scratch. Try this exercise:

> A regional grocer has seen profits fall for three quarters in a row despite stable revenue. Build a profit tree for the diagnosis.

<IssueTreeEmbed slug="orientation-warmup" height={380} />

A first-pass structure should use <Framework slug="profitability">profit = revenue − cost</Framework> as the root, then branch into volume × price on one side and variable × fixed on the other. There's no single right answer — that's the point of the exercise.
