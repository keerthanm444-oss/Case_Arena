## Why preparation is its own module

The M0–M7 modules give you a knowledge stack. They don't, by themselves, turn that knowledge into reliable performance. The translation layer — the routines, drill schedules, partner work, mock cycles — is what M8 covers.

> Reading a module is roughly 1/10th as valuable as completing one focused mock against it. Most candidates over-invest in reading and under-invest in mocks. The fix is mechanical: schedule the mocks.

The principles below are pulled from the standard recommendations across the canonical references<Citation publisher="Case in Point" title="Case in Point — Complete Case Interview Preparation" authors={['Cosentino, Marc']} year={2023} /><Citation publisher="Case Interview Secrets" title="Case Interview Secrets" authors={['Cheng, Victor']} year={2012} />. None of them are exotic; the discipline of actually following them is the rare thing.

## The three layers of prep

Effective prep has three concurrent layers:

1. **Daily drills** — 15–30 minutes, every day, on a single mechanical skill (mental math, slide titles, framework identification).
2. **Weekly mocks** — 60–90 minutes, 2–3 times per week, full-case simulations with a partner.
3. **Periodic full-rounds** — 2–3 hours, weekly, end-to-end at competition pace (read → structure → analyze → present → Q&A).

Each layer feeds the next. Daily drills build automaticity that frees working memory during mocks. Mocks build the pattern recognition that makes full-rounds feel familiar. Full-rounds build the stamina + consistency that real performance demands.

Skip a layer and the others compound poorly. Mocks without drills mean the math chokes; drills without full-rounds mean the stamina collapses on competition day.

## Daily drills — the 30-minute routine

Pick exactly one of these each day. Rotate across the week.

### Routine A — Mental math (15 min)

Run the <a href="/tools/mental-math">mental math drill</a>. Settings: medium level, all operations, 15 problems. Target: >85% accuracy, average time under 12 seconds per problem.

When that's automatic, escalate to hard or insane. Track your accuracy + average time in the dashboard; the trend is informative even when day-to-day fluctuation isn't.

### Routine B — Framework identification (15 min)

Run the <a href="/tools/framework-quiz">framework quiz</a>. Settings: 10 questions, mixed difficulty. Target: 8/10 correct, with confident framework selection in under 20 seconds per prompt.

The deeper goal: stop "thinking about which framework" and start instantly recognizing the prompt's shape (profitability vs market-entry vs M&A vs sizing).

### Routine C — Slide title craft (15 min)

Take any deck you've recently produced (or one from a public source). For each slide title, run it through the <a href="/tools/slide-critique">slide critique tool</a>. Rewrite the failing titles. Aim to get every title to **action-claim with quantification** in under 90 seconds per title.

This is the highest-leverage drill for presentation cases. Almost no one does it consistently; doing it twice a week is a real edge.

### Routine D — Structuring sprints (20 min)

Pick 4 case prompts from the library. For each, build the issue tree in the <a href="/tools/issue-tree">issue tree tool</a> in under 4 minutes. Don't try to solve the case — just structure it. Goal: 4 structures in 16 minutes, with two minutes spare to review.

The pattern this builds: structure-then-analyze becomes structure-then-analyze, not analyze-while-structuring. The two are different cognitive modes; mixing them slows both.

### Routine E — Decision under uncertainty (20 min)

Take a single high-stakes decision (from a case or a real-world business decision in the news). Build the decision tree in the <a href="/tools/decision-tree">decision tree tool</a> with probabilities + payoffs. Then run the EV calculation and identify the optimal path.

This is the most underused drill. Most case prep emphasizes structure + math but skips formal decision-under-uncertainty reasoning — even though the most senior judges probe for exactly this.

## Weekly mocks — the partner work protocol

Mocks with a partner are the highest-bandwidth prep there is. Two principles drive the protocol:

**Principle 1 — the partner runs the case strictly, not lovingly.** The single most damaging mock pattern is a kind partner who lets you off easy. They congratulate the good moves and skim past the weak ones; you leave feeling good and learning nothing. The fix: instruct your partner explicitly to be harder on you than the real judge would be.

**Principle 2 — debrief lasts as long as the case.** A 30-minute case deserves 30 minutes of debrief. Most pairs run cases for 30 minutes and debrief for 5. The 5-minute debrief catches the obvious mistakes; the next 25 minutes catch the subtle ones.

### The mock protocol

```
00:00 — 02:00    Partner reads the prompt aloud, you take notes
02:00 — 04:00    Clarifying questions — partner answers ONLY what's in the case
04:00 — 06:00    Structuring out loud, partner pushes back if MECE breaks
06:00 — 22:00    Analysis + drive — partner inserts 2-3 curveballs at random
22:00 — 28:00    Recommendation + Q&A — partner asks the 3 hardest questions
28:00 — 30:00    Hard stop. No bonus time.

30:00 — 60:00    Debrief:
                 - What worked? (5 min)
                 - What didn't? (10 min)
                 - One specific drill for the next session (5 min)
                 - Partner reverses role and you run them through a case (10 min)
```

If you only do one of these per week, do it well. Two per week is the sweet spot for most learners; three per week is the cap before fatigue erodes quality.

<Callout tone="tip" title="The single-skill mock variant">
  Every 3rd or 4th mock, run a deliberately constrained version: "we're only practicing the recommendation phase today." Skip structuring, hand the partner a worked tree, let them ask you to deliver the synthesis with three different audiences. Constrained mocks build specific moves faster than full ones.
</Callout>

## Full rounds — the periodic stamina build

A full round is end-to-end at competition pace. For interviews: 30–45 minutes per case, 3–4 cases back-to-back with 10-minute breaks. For competitions: simulate the full deliverable cycle (24-hour case in 4 hours of focused work; 1-week case in 2 days).

The point of a full round isn't to learn new skills. It's to **build endurance and consistency**. The skills you bring are the skills you have; the question is whether they hold up at hour 3 of back-to-back cases.

Schedule one full round every weekend for the 4 weeks before a real event. Treat the schedule as immovable — moving it routinely is the biggest single tell of unserious prep.

## A 6-week prep plan

A standard ramp before a major competition or interview round looks like this:

### Weeks 1–2 — foundation

- Daily routine A or B (rotate by day)
- 2 mocks per week, slower pace, generous clarifying-question budget
- 1 full round on weekend 2

**Goal:** internalize M0–M3 (orient → structure → analyze → synthesize). At the end of week 2, the four canonical frameworks should be automatic.

### Weeks 3–4 — fluency

- Daily routine A + B (interleaved)
- 3 mocks per week, normal pace, normal clarification budget
- 1 full round each weekend

**Goal:** internalize M4–M5 (delivery + math). At end of week 4, no mental math should take >15 seconds; slide titles should default to action-claims.

### Weeks 5–6 — performance

- Daily routine: mix of all five (C and D become higher-priority)
- 3 mocks per week, harder partner, fewer clarifications allowed
- 1 full round each weekend; the week-6 round at full competition pace

**Goal:** internalize M6–M7 (psychology + format awareness). At end of week 6, recovery moves should be automatic and the competition format you're prepping for should feel familiar.

This ramp is calibrated for someone with the M0–M7 reading done. Without the reading, the ramp doesn't compound; with it, the 6 weeks produces a real performance change.

## Partner selection

Three patterns for finding mock partners, ranked by typical effectiveness:

1. **Same-stage peers in a focused study group.** 3-4 people at similar skill levels, committed to a fixed schedule, rotating who runs cases. Highest signal-to-noise; the social commitment enforces the discipline.

2. **Senior peers who've already done the round you're prepping for.** Lower bandwidth (they're harder to schedule) but they catch subtle mistakes you'd miss. One mock per week with a senior peer plus two with same-stage is a strong combination.

3. **Coaches / paid mock partners.** Useful for credibility-check before a high-stakes event but unnecessary for the routine prep cycle. The marginal value of a paid mock over a free same-stage mock is small if your same-stage partner is competent.

Avoid: solo "self-mocks" where you imagine a partner's questions. They feel productive but they miss the actual hard part of a mock, which is the unexpected probe.

## Tracking progress

The <a href="/dashboard">progress dashboard</a> tracks streak days, drill scores, and recently-viewed pages — local-only, no account needed. Two things worth watching weekly:

- **Drill score trend.** If your mental math accuracy is stuck below 85% after 2 weeks of daily practice, your routine isn't working; change something. Either the difficulty level is wrong or the volume is too low.

- **Streak length.** Streaks > 14 days correlate strongly with sustained skill gains. Streaks that frequently break at 3-5 days correlate with stop-start prep that doesn't compound. If you're in the latter pattern, halve the daily commitment until you can sustain it.

The dashboard is a feedback loop, not a scoreboard. Watch the trends; ignore individual data points.

## Common failure modes

Five recurring failures across observed prep cycles:

1. **All reading, no mocks.** Reading the modules is necessary; it's not sufficient. The translation from knowledge to skill happens in mocks, not in reading.

2. **Mocks without debriefs.** A 30-minute case with a 5-minute "how'd I do?" debrief captures maybe 20% of the learning available. Schedule the debrief as a hard 30 minutes.

3. **Skipping the math drill because "I'm good at math."** Almost everyone says this. Almost no one is at the speed needed for live cases. Drill anyway; you'll see.

4. **Cramming the week before.** Sleep-deprived performance under pressure beats well-rested performance under pressure ~0% of the time. Front-load the work; coast the final week.

5. **Solo prep until the very end.** Mocks teach things solo work can't. Find a partner by week 2 of any prep cycle.

## Self-test

Audit the next 7 days of your current prep:

1. How many daily drill sessions are scheduled? (Target: 5–7)
2. How many mocks are scheduled? (Target: 2–3)
3. Is there a full round on the weekend? (Target: yes, if you're <4 weeks from an event)
4. For each scheduled mock, do you know the case + the partner + the debrief slot? (Target: yes for all)
5. If you missed every session this week, what would force you to make them up? (Target: a real consequence — accountability partner, scheduled commitment)

If you scored less than 4/5, the prep is informal. Either accept that and lower expectations, or formalize the schedule.
