## How to use this list

M11 is reference, not curriculum. Drop in for a specific gap; don't read top-to-bottom. Every entry below is real, verifiable, and cited; the books are linked from the <a href="/resources">resources hub page</a> with the interactive filter.

Three principles for adding resources to your prep stack:

1. **Depth before breadth.** One canonical reference read thoroughly beats five surface-read recommendations. Pick a book, finish it, then evaluate whether you need another.
2. **Practice over consumption.** A podcast or YouTube video can extend your knowledge by 5% per hour. A mock case extends your skill by ~20% per hour. The hour ratio matters.
3. **Vet aggressively.** The case-prep content market is large and uneven. Anything not on the canonical list below should be filtered through a single question: **does this author have skin in the game?** Authors who've placed candidates, judged competitions, or worked the relevant role get a default benefit of the doubt; pure aggregators don't.

## The four canonical books

These four are the foundation. If you only invest in this section, invest here.

### Case in Point — Marc Cosentino

<Citation publisher="Case in Point" title="Case in Point — Complete Case Interview Preparation" authors={['Cosentino, Marc']} year={2023} />

**What it covers:** the full case-interview prep stack — frameworks, sample cases, sizing methodology. The default starter book for case interviews.

**Read it when:** you're starting from zero on case interviews specifically. The framework chapter alone is worth the price.

**Don't expect:** depth on slide design or communication. Cosentino is light on the presentation side; pair with Minto.

**Time investment:** ~10–15 hours for a thorough read. ~3 hours for a skim-and-extract.

### The Pyramid Principle — Barbara Minto

<Citation publisher="The Pyramid Principle" title="The Pyramid Principle: Logic in Writing and Thinking" authors={['Minto, Barbara']} year={2009} />

**What it covers:** structured thinking and answer-first communication. Originally developed at McKinsey in the 1970s; every consulting firm of consequence has since trained on a version of it.

**Read it when:** your synthesis (M3) is unconvincing. If judges keep saying "I'm not sure what you're recommending", Minto is the cure.

**Don't expect:** a quick read. Minto is dense; the principles compound but they don't surface easily. Plan multiple passes over weeks.

**Time investment:** ~20 hours for a deep read with exercises. Worth every hour if synthesis is your gap.

### Case Interview Secrets — Victor Cheng

<Citation publisher="Case Interview Secrets" title="Case Interview Secrets" authors={['Cheng, Victor']} year={2012} />

**What it covers:** the mechanics of structuring an unfamiliar problem, with stronger emphasis on hypothesis-driven structures than Cosentino. Cheng was an ex-McKinsey hire-then-interviewer; his perspective is more interviewer-side.

**Read it when:** Cosentino feels too template-driven and you want to develop hypothesis-driven structuring instincts. Pairs well with M1.

**Don't expect:** as much breadth as Cosentino on the frameworks themselves. Cheng goes deeper on fewer moves.

**Time investment:** ~8–12 hours.

### Competitive Strategy — Michael Porter

<Citation publisher="Competitive Strategy" title="Competitive Strategy: Techniques for Analyzing Industries and Competitors" authors={['Porter, Michael E.']} year={1980} />

**What it covers:** Five Forces, generic strategies (cost leadership / differentiation / focus), industry analysis. The original 1980 academic treatment that every subsequent strategy framework descends from.

**Read it when:** you want depth on the strategy side — particularly Five Forces, which is a recurring framework in market-entry and M&A cases.

**Don't expect:** approachability. Porter is academic; he's written for graduate students and consultants, not first-year case-preppers. Read it selectively.

**Time investment:** ~15–25 hours for a thorough read; can excerpt the Five Forces chapter standalone for ~2 hours of focused study.

### Lords of Strategy — Walter Kiechel

<Citation publisher="Lords of Strategy" title="Lords of Strategy: The Secret Intellectual History of the New Corporate World" authors={['Kiechel, Walter']} year={2010} />

**What it covers:** the intellectual history of strategy consulting — the rise of BCG, McKinsey, and Bain; the development of the BCG matrix, experience curve, and other core consulting concepts.

**Read it when:** you want context on **why** the frameworks exist. Doesn't directly improve case performance but gives you the vocabulary + history that comes up in conversations with senior interviewers.

### Crafting Persuasion

<Citation publisher="Crafting Persuasion" title="Crafting Persuasion" />

**What it covers:** strategic communication and persuasive document craft. Complements Minto on the writing + slide side.

**Read it when:** you've finished Minto and want a more contemporary perspective on the same skill set.

## Firm publications

The consulting firms publish their own thinking. Selectively useful — read the topical pieces relevant to your case domains, skip the marketing.

- **McKinsey & Company**<Citation publisher="McKinsey & Company" title="McKinsey Quarterly" /> — *McKinsey Quarterly* is the public-facing publication. Industry-specific insights, often well-cited.

- **Boston Consulting Group**<Citation publisher="Boston Consulting Group" title="BCG Insights" /> — *BCG Insights* publication. Strong on the strategy + transformation side.

- **Bain & Company**<Citation publisher="Bain & Company" title="Bain Insights" /> — strong on private equity and consumer products topics.

- **Deloitte**<Citation publisher="Deloitte" title="Deloitte Insights" /> — *Deloitte Insights*. Broad coverage; particularly strong on technology and government.

- **Oliver Wyman**<Citation publisher="Oliver Wyman" title="Oliver Wyman Forum" />, **Roland Berger**<Citation publisher="Roland Berger" title="Roland Berger Insights" />, **Kearney**<Citation publisher="Kearney" title="Kearney Insights" /> — smaller scale but useful for specific topics.

How to use firm publications: pick the firms whose interviewing style you're prepping for, then read their thinking on the industries you'll likely be asked about. A McKinsey-targeted candidate prepping for healthcare cases should read 3-4 recent McKinsey Quarterly healthcare pieces — not as case practice but as **industry vocabulary**.

## Podcasts, channels, communities — a vetting framework

Outside the canonical sources above, the prep content market is large and quality is uneven. Rather than recommend specific podcasts or channels (which change rapidly and would require ongoing verification), here's a vetting framework you can apply to anything you find:

### The four vetting questions

1. **Who is the host's track record?** Have they been a consultant at a top firm, judged competitions, or placed candidates? Or are they an aggregator with no operating background?

2. **Is the content specific or generic?** Specific frameworks with worked examples beat generic "tips for case interviews" by an order of magnitude. If the title is "how to ace your case interview", expect generic. If the title is "how to structure a market-entry case for a B2B SaaS company", expect specific.

3. **Does the resource cite its sources?** Resources that cite real published cases + reference texts are using the same discipline you're trying to learn. Resources that assert "85% of candidates fail because…" without citation are fabricating.

4. **Is it free, paid, or freemium — and does the price model align incentives?** Free content from a firm aligns with "we want you to come work here." Paid content aligns with "we want you to recommend us to your peers." Freemium often misaligns — the free content is bait, the paid content is the substance, but you can't verify quality before buying.

If a resource scores ≥3 yes on these four questions, it's likely worth your time. If it scores 0 or 1, skip.

### Communities worth knowing about

The canonical case-competition + case-interview communities tend to live around:

- **University-specific consulting clubs** (most undergrad business schools have one). Local, networked, and the highest-bandwidth way to find mock partners. If your school doesn't have one, the next-closest one usually accepts outside members.

- **Competition-specific Slack/Discord channels** for the major competitions (HULT Prize, KPMG, John Molson, etc.). Look for these on the competition's website; they're how teams find peers in past years' competitions.

- **Subreddit communities** centered on consulting careers. Lower signal-to-noise than focused clubs but useful for sourcing study partners across geographies.

Treat communities as **mock partner sources**, not content sources. The content recommendations in any open community are usually mediocre at best.

## A starter resource path

If you're starting from zero and want a concrete sequence:

**Week 1:** Read Cosentino's framework chapter. Map the canonical frameworks onto the M1 module.

**Week 2:** Read Minto's first three chapters. Apply the Pyramid Principle to one practice case synthesis.

**Week 3:** Read 3 HBR articles in your target industry. Build industry vocabulary.

**Week 4:** Skim the Five Forces chapter of Porter. Run the Forces analysis on one competitive-strategy case.

**Week 5+:** Pure case practice + targeted reading driven by gaps that emerge in mocks.

The first four weeks of reading = the foundation. After that, marginal value of reading drops sharply; marginal value of mocks rises sharply. Don't over-invest in the reading phase.

## Common resource-investment failure modes

Five patterns that recur:

1. **Buying every prep book on the market.** Five half-read books are worth less than one fully-read book. Commit; finish.

2. **Watching prep videos as a substitute for mocks.** Passive video consumption feels productive but builds little skill. The mock partner ratio matters.

3. **Joining communities and never posting.** Communities work via reciprocity. Lurking-only gets you weak signal.

4. **Reading firm publications without picking firms.** General firm content is mostly marketing. Industry-specific content from the firm you're targeting is the part that's actually useful.

5. **Treating the resource list as a checklist to "complete".** Resources serve specific gaps. Map the resource to the gap; don't accumulate resources for their own sake.
