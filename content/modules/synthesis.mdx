## The handoff from analysis to recommendation

You've built a tree (M1). You've produced numbers (M2). Now you have to **say** something — to a judge, a CEO, a board. Synthesis is the discipline of saying the right thing first, supported by the right evidence, in a way the listener will remember.

This is the single largest area where strong performers separate from average ones. Almost any candidate can do passable analysis on a familiar framework. Fewer can finish a 6-minute case and deliver an answer the judge actually buys.

> A recommendation buried under context is not a recommendation. It's a guess the listener has to dig out.

The dominant reference for everything in this module is Barbara Minto's <em>The Pyramid Principle</em>.<Citation publisher="The Pyramid Principle" title="The Pyramid Principle: Logic in Writing and Thinking" authors={['Minto, Barbara']} year={2009} /> The book was developed at McKinsey in the 1970s; every consulting firm of consequence has since trained on a version of it. Internalize the moves below and you have ~80% of what you need for any case synthesis.

## The Pyramid Principle in 90 seconds

The structure is a tree, top-down:

1. **Top of pyramid** — one sentence, one claim. The recommendation.
2. **Middle of pyramid** — typically three supports. Each is a defensible statement on its own.
3. **Bottom of pyramid** — the evidence beneath each support. Numbers, comparisons, citations.

You deliver top-down: lead with the recommendation, then walk through the supports, then drill into evidence only when prompted. Hostile audiences forgive short confident answers; they punish meandering ones.

A worked example from a market-entry case (using the framework from M1):

> **Recommendation:** Enter market X in Q3 2026 via partnership with a local incumbent. Cap initial investment at $5M.
>
> **Three supports:**
>
> 1. The addressable opportunity is large enough to matter (~$200M ARR at year 5 in our base case).
> 2. Our capabilities map to ~60% of what's needed, with the partnership covering the rest.
> 3. The downside is bounded — the $5M cap means a failed entry costs us 1% of annual revenue.

Notice three things about that synthesis:

- The recommendation is a **complete sentence with a verb**: "Enter market X". Not "Market X consideration" or "Entry analysis".
- Each support is **independently defensible**. If support 2 collapsed, the recommendation might survive on supports 1 + 3.
- The supports cover **opportunity, capability, and risk** — three different dimensions, not three flavors of the same point.

## Answer-first writing

The single discipline that separates Minto-fluent communicators from everyone else is: **lead with the answer**.

Compare these two openers:

> **Burying:** "We looked at three options. Option A had pros and cons. Option B had different pros and cons. Option C — which we'll discuss in more detail in the next section — is also worth considering. After weighing them carefully, we recommend Option B."

> **Leading:** "We recommend Option B. Three reasons: it's the only option that protects the existing customer base, the only one we can ship in under 6 months, and the only one within the budget cap."

The second version respects the listener's time. It commits. It can be **contested** — the listener can immediately ask "are you sure about the 6 months?" — which is what you want, because that's where the real conversation happens.

### When you genuinely don't know yet

Sometimes the analysis isn't conclusive. Say so explicitly, and structure your "I don't know" with the same discipline:

> "We have not converged on a recommendation. The decision hinges on whether assumption X holds; we recommend a 30-day diligence sprint to validate X before committing capital. If X is true, the path is A; if false, the path is B."

That's an honest non-answer that still has the Pyramid shape. The judge sees structured uncertainty, not flailing.

## The "so what" test

Every claim in your synthesis should pass a single question: **so what?**

"Revenue grew 12% YoY." So what?

"Revenue grew 12% YoY, faster than the market's 8%, indicating we gained share." Better — now there's an implication.

"Revenue grew 12% YoY, faster than the market's 8%, indicating we gained share — which means our acquisition strategy is working and we should accelerate it." Better still — there's an implication and an action.

Apply the so-what test to every support in your pyramid. If a support doesn't lead to action, it's data, not synthesis.

  Try writing your supports as **slide titles**. "Revenue grew 12% YoY" is a descriptive title — it tells the reader nothing about what to do. "Revenue gains share at twice market rate, supporting acquisition strategy" is an action title — it makes a claim and points at action. ## Communicating uncertainty without hedging

Strong synthesis names uncertainty **precisely**, not vaguely. Compare:

- ❌ "We think it might be around $5 million, perhaps."
- ✅ "We estimate $5 million, with a $3-7M range based on the adoption-rate assumption."

The second one carries the same uncertainty but reads as confident analysis instead of hedging. The trick: give a point estimate, then state the range and the driver.

### The hedging words to avoid

These words signal weakness without conveying real information:

- "Perhaps", "maybe", "possibly"
- "We think", "we believe"
- "It seems like"
- "Could be argued"
- "Somewhat", "fairly", "kind of"

If you're certain enough to make a claim, make it. If you're not, name the specific uncertainty (range, dependency, missing data) instead of hedging the whole sentence.

## Three classic synthesis shapes

Beyond the canonical Pyramid, three shapes recur:

### Situation → Complication → Question → Answer (SCQA)

Minto's classic narrative open.<Citation publisher="The Pyramid Principle" title="The Pyramid Principle: Logic in Writing and Thinking" authors={['Minto, Barbara']} year={2009} /> Open with the situation the listener already knows. Introduce the complication. State the question that complication raises. Then answer it.

> **Situation:** Our coffee business has been growing at 8% for five years. **Complication:** Margins are now compressing despite stable revenue. **Question:** What's driving the compression and what should we do? **Answer:** Input cost inflation, addressable in two ways…

SCQA works for openings; the body still uses the Pyramid.

### Recommendation + 3 supports + risks

The most common case-competition close. Lead with the recommendation, three supports, then **explicitly name 2-3 risks and how you'd mitigate them**. Showing you've thought about what could go wrong is what separates a credible recommendation from a glib one.

### Decision under uncertainty: "if X, then A; if Y, then B"

For cases where the recommendation depends on a single unknown, lay out the conditional structure cleanly:

> "If user-acquisition cost stays below $40, we should scale aggressively to 50 cities by 2027. If CAC trends above $50, we hold at the current 12 cities and reinvest in product before pushing growth."

This is more honest than picking one branch arbitrarily, and judges respect it more.

## Slide title craft

In a presentation case, every slide should have an **action title** — a claim, not a description.

- ❌ "Revenue trend"
- ✅ "Revenue grew 12%, driven by retail volume increase"

Action titles take an extra 30 seconds to write but **double the comprehension speed** of your deck. A reader can skim five action titles and get the argument; they can't get anything from five descriptive titles.

## Common failure modes

After watching enough synthesis sessions, the same five mistakes recur:

1. **Burying the recommendation.** Walking through analysis for 90 seconds before stating what to do. Lead. Always.

2. **Three supports that say the same thing.** "It's a good market, it has growth, and demand is strong." Those are three flavors of one point. Three supports should be on **three different dimensions** (e.g. opportunity, capability, risk).

3. **No quantification.** "Significant", "substantial", "meaningful" — these are placeholders for numbers you don't have. Either find the number or specify the dependency.

4. **No "so what".** Reporting facts without implications. Every claim needs to point at action.

5. **Hedge-stacking.** "We think it might possibly be around" stacks four hedges. Pick one (the range, the driver) and commit to the rest.

## Self-test

Take a case you've already worked on (Acme Coffee or Veridian, or one you've drilled on your own). Write the synthesis in this exact shape:

1. **One-sentence recommendation** with a verb.
2. **Three supports**, each independently defensible.
3. **One named risk** with one named mitigation.
4. **One falsifier** — what evidence would change your recommendation.

Total length: under 150 words. If you can't fit it under 150 words, your supports aren't tight enough yet.
